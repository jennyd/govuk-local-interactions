There are lots of ways to reduce energy use within the home by using both natural techniques to maximise sunlight and simple technological switches.

A 100w light bulb left on for just half an hour creates enough CO2 to fill a party balloon and households in the UK spend around Â£2.4bn every year lighting their homes.

Many improvements to save energy and money can be made in your home immediately and are absolutely free:

  • Turning lights off when rooms are unoccupied;
  • Cleaning light fittings;
  • Choosing pale colours for your walls and furniture can maximise the amount of light in your room;
  • Reducing lighting levels to a minimum recommended for the activities involved;
  • Making more use of natural light, especially in new building (ways to do this are explained further down the page).

Other changes have relatively small upfront costs and pay for themselves in a few months. For example replacing incandescent bulbs for low energy alternatives or fitting timers and movement sensors to reduce the amount of time that lighting is on.

Maximising natural lighting

The sun is the source of nearly all energy on Earth. There are a number of ways to make the most of the natural light that your home receives which do not necessarily involve structural alterations.

If designing or modifying your building, the orientation and size of the windows can affect the buildings lighting dramatically. Below is a list of factors which influence the amount of sunlight received through windows:

  • Window size;
  • Window angle â good orientation and layout allows you to make the most of sunlight both for warming and providing natural light to all rooms;
  • Weather, latitude and the seasons â in northern latitudes of the UK the sun passes lower in the sky than in the south. Solar radiation enters the atmosphere at a more oblique angle and a greater proportion is absorbed or reflected before it reaches the surface; 
  • Orientation â a southerly orientation maximises solar gain in winter and limits summer overheating due to the high angle of the midday sun;
  • Window design â a window should have varying sizes of opening to allow ventilation to be varied without compromising security;

Solar tubes are also another way of increasing the amount of light in your home without excessive cost or extensive alterations. They comprise of a dome on the roof which collects sunlight and directs it into a reflective tube that runs through the rafters.

The light is then directed through a diffuser in the ceiling spreading light throughout the room whilst looking like a typical light fixture, thus allowing light into the room without requiring electricity.

They are relatively easy to install in existing homes, and cost significantly less than traditional skylights.
