Looking after the residents of Central Bedfordshire

Carbon Monoxide awareness

Households with fuel (gas, oil, LPG, coal) burning appliances should be aware of the dangers of faulty appliances or of using those appliances incorrectly. Incomplete combustion of the fuel can lead to the poisonous gas, carbon monoxide being emitted in harmful amounts. Symptoms from low-level carbon monoxide poisoning can sometimes be confused with other illness symptoms but high levels can kill.

Derelict properties - public safety

When buildings are unoccupied or the occupier is temporarily absent, and the building is either not effectively secured against unauthorised entry or is likely to become a danger to public health then the council may serve notice under Section 29 of the Local Government (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1982 to remedy the situation.

Accumulations of waste at residential premises

Accumulations of waste at residential premises


Problems with accumulations of waste are both varied and widespread. We are not able to take action in every case; however, the following will give an overview of the instances where we may be able to help.


Accumulations affecting public health


Some waste items can pose a threat to public health. When we receive complaints about accumulations of waste that are 'prejudicial to health' we contact the owner or occupier of the land where it is situated asking them to arrange for it to be removed.
Please note that the definition of 'prejudicial to health' within this legislation does not include physical injury caused by items such as broken glass or trip hazards such as piles of rubble.

Accumulations giving rise to a nuisance (e.g. odour)


The council can take action in cases where accumulations or deposits are either prejudicial to health or a nuisance.

Accumulations providing a food source or harbourage for rodents


When the council receives a complaint about rodent infestations or waste capable of attracting rodents an officer will contact the owners or occupiers of the land in question asking them to remove the waste and take any other measures needed to keep their land free from rats or mice.

Accumulations giving rise to rodent infestations within buildings


In extreme cases where waste items within a property have led to an infestation within the building itself, we may be able to use public health legislation to deal with it as a verminous property.

Accumulations of inert waste


It is fairly common for councils to receive complaints about waste at domestic premises considered to be an eyesore. Except in certain extreme cases where enforcement officers may be able to take action for breaches of planning legislation there is little that the council can do where neither foodstuff nor hazardous waste is present, and there is no evidence of vermin (rats, mice, insects etc) infestation

Drains and sewers

Responsibilities for sewerage systems


Water and sewerage companies in England and Wales are responsible for private sewers.


Not all private pipes are included. There are some cases where the property owners remain responsible for the sections of pipe between the property / building and the transferred private sewer or lateral drain.


Problems that appear to relate to shared pipework and/or pipework beyond your boundary are likely to involve the public sewerage system. In such cases you will need to contact the sewerage company that deals with your area:

  • Anglian Water (cover most of Central Bedfordshire) – 0800 145 145
  • Thames Water (cover a small area of southern Central Bedfordshire) – 0845 9200 800


Problems relating to private drainage (including cesspits and septic tanks) will be the responsibility of the homeowners. Should this be the case then you may need to contact a drainage contractor or plumber if it is not something that you can resolve yourself.

If you have any queries please contact us on 0300 300 8007 or at housingsolutions@centralbedfordshire.gov.uk. 

Tenants may be able to obtain assistance from their landlords.

What can the council do with regards to overflowing cesspits and septic tanks?


We can serve notice under Section 50 of the Public Health Act 1936 on the occupiers of premises that drain into cesspits and septic tanks if they are overflowing.

Filthy and verminous properties

We will become involved if there are public health risks

Some people find it difficult looking after themselves and their property.

This may lead to the property becoming untidy and dirty, and whilst not ideal this may not be serious enough for the council to become involved. However, in some cases this may progress to infestations of rats, mice, flies etc or accumulations of filth. Should conditions deteriorate to this level then the council would become involved due to the associated public health risks.

Becoming aware


We often become aware of such cases by referral from Social Services or housing officers (both council and housing association), If you are aware of a case that may require our attention then please contact us on 0300 300 8007.

Once we have been notified of a problem such as this we will arrange to inspect the property in order to identify works that may be necessary.

Notice served


If appropriate a notice could be served on the occupier or owner requiring the necessary work to be carried out within a specified timescale.
