Reducing my Bill - Discounts and Exemptions

Find out if you need to pay Council Tax or if you're entitled for money off your bill

Exemptions - who doesn't have to pay Council Tax?

In some cases you do not have to pay Council Tax as you are classed as "exempt". There are a number of different cases that qualify for Council Tax exemption, and each is given a letter. These classes are set by Government regulations which we must follow. Some classes apply to properties where someone is not living (an unoccupied property) and others apply to an occupied property where someone is living.

Exemption classes for unoccupied properties apply because they are:

  • Owned by a charity (Class B). This is only allowed for up to six months
  • Left empty by someone who has gone to prison (Class D). This is unless they are in prison for not paying their Council Tax or a fine.
  • Previously occupied by a person who now lives in permanent residential care (Class E)
  • Empty because the liable person has died (Class F). This means their executors or personal representatives are now liable. This is allowed for up to six months after probate is granted.
  • Not allowed to be lived in by law (Class G)
  • Waiting to be occupied by a minister of religion (Class H)
  • Empty because the liable person has moved to receive personal care because of old age, disability or illness (Class I)
  • Left empty because someone has moved out to give personal care to another person (Class J)
  • Owned by a student, and was last occupied by a student (Class K)
  • Being repossessed by a Mortgage Lender (Class L)
  • The responsibility of a bankrupt's estate (Class Q)
  • A site for an individual caravan, mobile home or mooring (Class R)
  • Difficult to let because they are linked to, or in grounds of another property (Class T)

Exemption classes for occupied properties apply because they are:

  • A Student hall of Residence (Class M)
  • A property only occupied by students (Class N)
  • At least one liable person is a member of a visiting force (Class P)
  • Occupied by residents who are all under 18 years old (Class S)
  • Occupied by residents who are all severely mentally impaired (Class U)
  • Occupied by at least one foreign diplomat (Class V)
  • A "Granny Annexe"(Class W)

If you own a fully exempt dwelling, you will receive a bill to let you know which valuation band the property has been placed in, and what the Council Tax would be if it were not exempt. The exemption will be clearly shown on the bill. If the Council writes to tell you it believes your property is exempt, but you realise that it should not be, you must contact the council and tell them - otherwise you may face a penalty.

If the Council decides your dwelling is not exempt and you disagree, you should contact the Council saying why you think your property should be exempt. If you still disagree with the Council, you can appeal to a Valuation Tribunal. You should continue to pay your original bill while your appeal is outstanding.

Discounts - can I get any money off my Council Tax?

The full Council Tax bill assumes that there are two adults living in a dwelling. If only one adult lives in a dwelling as their main home, the Council Tax bill is reduced by 25%. This is known as single person discount. Other discounts include:

Disabled Band Reduction

Your bill may be reduced if a permanently disabled person (whether they are an adult or a child) lives in the dwelling. They do not have to be responsible for paying the Council Tax bill.  You can apply for a disabled reduction by completing an Application for Disabled Band Reduction. 

The Disabled Band Reduction scheme aims to ensure that disabled people do not pay more Council Tax because they live in a larger property than they would have needed if they were not disabled. However, having a disability does not automatically entitle you to a reduction. The requirements for a reduction are that the property must be the main residence of at least one disabled person, and it must have at least one of the following:

  • An additional bathroom or kitchen
  • A room (not being a bathroom, kitchen or lavatory) which is mainly used by the disabled person
  • Enough space for the use of a wheelchair - if the wheelchair is for outdoor use only, this will not count.

The room must also be essential or of major importance to the disabled person's well-being, due to the nature and extent of their disability. An extra room does not need to have been specially built, but your home will not qualify for a reduction unless the 'essential or of major' importance test is met. Simply rearranging rooms (e.g. having a bedroom on the ground floor rather than the first floor) is unlikely to make your home eligible for a reduction.

If your home is eligible, your bill will be reduced to that of a property in the next Council Tax band down. For example, a Band D property will be charged at a Band rate. Even if your property is in Band A (the lowest band) you will still receive a reduction. It will be the same in cash terms as the reductions for homes in Band B, or D.

Family Annexes (that are not exempt)

For family annexes that are not exempt, there is a new Council Tax discount of 50% for people living in annexes, provided they are related to the person liable to pay the Council Tax of the main dwellings. The discount is extended to those people living in dwellings with annexes which are unoccupied, provided they are using those annexes as part of their main residence. If a non-family member occupies an annexe then the discount will not apply.

If you think that any of the above applies to you, contact us and we will send you the relevant forms.
