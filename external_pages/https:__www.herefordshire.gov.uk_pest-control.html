Pest control

Our team offers a pest control service for domestic and commercial premises. We can provide one-off treatments and an ongoing contract service.

Our pest control technicians operate flexible working from Monday to Friday, and it may be possible for them to call outside of business hours. We will also offer weekend visits during a busy wasp season.

Our technicians will provide you with advice on preventing re-infestation.

We are members of the National Pest Technicians Association (NPTA) and the British Pest Control Association (BPCA). All our full-time officers hold the Royal Society of Public Health's pest control certificate and have been CRB checked for their current positions in the council and are eligible to work in establishments with children and vulnerable adults.

Our technicians’ continuous professional development is monitored by BASIS Prompt to ensure they are up to date with all current pest control legislation.

What pests do you deal with?

  • Ants
  • Bed bugs
  • Bees
  • Carpet beetles
  • Carpet moths
  • Cluster flies
  • Cockroaches
  • Fleas
  • Harlequin ladybirds
  • Moles
  • Rats and mice
  • Wasps and hornets

Ants






Ants can have an impact on the safety of food, by contaminating food handling surfaces.

Bed bugs






Outbreaks of bed bugs have dramatically increased over the last 10 years. These can be found even in the cleanest homes and hotels.

Adults are 4-5mm long, red to brown in colour, oval, and possess an objectionable smell.

Bees






Bumble bees - We can treat problems with bumble bees, mortar bees and miner bees.







Honey bees - The British Beekeepers Association will be able to supply the number of a local bee keeper who is able to remove swarming honey bees.

Carpet beetles






Adult carpet beetles range from 1.7 mm to 3.5 mm in length. The body is rounded, almost spherical. They have an irregular pattern of white, brownish and yellowish patches formed by scales of different colours.

Carpet moths






The larvae (woolly bear) feed on animal skin, bird nests, pellets, fur, clothing and floor and furniture covering made of animal skin.

The moths complete their cycles within a year and are more like webbing clothes moths in that they spin webbing in areas where they like to reside. Their speed of development depends entirely upon local temperature, humidity and food supplies.

Cluster flies






These creatures are up to 10mm in length, with a distinctive dark grey/olive colour. They will spend winters in buildings, sometimes in very large numbers.

Cockroaches






Cockroaches feed on a wide range of organic matter, from conventional food to faeces and even other cockroaches.

Cockroaches are very hardy and resilient to attempts at pest control. They mainly come out at night, hiding in crevices during the day.

Cockroaches may contaminate food directly and even trigger an allergic reaction in some people.

Fleas






Fleas are found in dwellings where pets are kept or in areas where feral cats live.

The adults are 2-3mm long, red to brown in colour, with flattened bodies.

Adult fleas are stimulated by vibrations and carbon dioxide production. This is why hordes of fleas sometimes attack people entering houses which have been empty for a long time.

Fleas act as a host of the dog tapeworm which can also infect humans.

Harlequin ladybirds






These are larger than most ladybirds. They tend to spend winter indoors, may bite humans and leave an unpleasant odour or stain when frightened.

Moles






Moles can be found wherever there is suitable soil for tunnelling. They usually avoid soil that is shallow, stony, waterlogged or acidic.

They are usually active just after sunrise and just before sunset. Moles damage plants by tunnelling, deface grasslands and gardens with their molehill, and can also cause silage crops to be spoiled.

When you contact us regarding a problem with moles, an estimate of the scale of the mole infestation will establish the cost of treatment. After this, a pest control officer will call you to arrange an appointment.

Rats and mice






Rats breed rapidly, are very adaptable and highly mobile. This combination can make rat control a difficult task for the untrained individual.

Mice can at times be vermin, damaging and eating crops, causing structural damage and spreading diseases through their parasites and faeces.

Many of the traps available on the commercial market are unsafe to use in buildings where children live or study.  Our officers use bait boxes which are specially designed to entice rats or mice inside whiles ensuring none of the bait falls out. The bait used is in blocks which are wired inside the box in separate compartments. The boxes are also designed to ensure they cannot be opened by children or other animals.

Wasps and hornets






Wasps begin building their nests each spring. It is important to treat nests as soon as possible.

Wasps will not use an old nest again, but may build a new one close to it.

Hornets are larger than the common wasp, but are normally much less aggressive.

Please note: we do not remove dead nests.

What pests don't you deal with?

We do not provide a pest control service for bats, foxes, or grey squirrels but we provide the information below about treatment or advice.

  • Bats
  • Foxes
  • Grey squirrels
  • Pigeons
  • Seagulls

Bats

All bats in the UK are protected due to their declining numbers. If you have problems with bats, contact the Bat Conservation Trust.

Foxes

There is no doubt that foxes can cause considerable nuisance. However, many people enjoy the experience of encountering the urban fox, even to the extent of deliberately feeding and encouraging them.

Foxes can carry fleas and internal parasites such as worms, but there is little evidence that these can be passed on to humans or their pets. Foxes also carry toxocara, a disease which can also be carried by domestic dogs and cats.

Like most local councils, we do not control the fox population, as it is expensive and often counterproductive. More foxes do not breed each year and killing foxes can actually upset breeding patterns, leading to increased activity and disruption.

To reduce fox activity on your property:

  • Keep your rubbish in bins until the day of collection wherever practical
  • Make sure your bins have secured lids
  • Use special bird feeders rather than putting food on the floor
  • Do not feed your dogs and cats out of doors
  • Do not use bone meal fertilisers in the garden
  • Use an appropriate repellent from a DIY store
  • If you keep smaller pets in your garden, ensure they have strong cages

Grey squirrels

To eradicate grey squirrels:

  • Use a squirrel proof bird feeder
  • A pest control company may help you squirrel proof your property and neighbouring trees

Pigeons 

Pigeons are a common sight in urban environments, and may also be found on farms. To deal with pigeons, take away the food source and pigeon proof the building to prevent entry. Whilst we will not eradicate pigeons, we can proof buildings/areas where pigeons roost. Free quotes are available.

Gulls in Herefordshire

Over the past 20 years, seagulls have moved inland to nest. In 2009, it was estimated that Hereford had approximately 400-500 breeding pairs of seagulls - specifically herring gulls and lesser black backed gulls.

The gulls mainly nest on the flat roofed factories in the north of Hereford city and in Rotherwas, although colonies have also spread to some of the city centre shops, houses and hotels. Commonly cited problems include: 

  • Noise caused by calling gulls and by their heavy footsteps
  • Mess caused by their droppings, fouling on washing, gardens and people
  • Damage to property, caused by gulls picking at roofing materials and by nests which block gutters or hold moisture against the building structure
  • Diving and swooping on people and pets. This usually occurs when chicks have fallen from a nest
  • Blockage of gas flues by nesting materials. This can have serious consequences if gas fumes are prevented from venting properly 

Legislation: seagull control

The principal legislation dealing with the control of birds is the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. Generally, it is illegal to capture, injure or destroy any wild bird or interfere with its nest or eggs. The penalties for disregarding the law can be severe. 

The law does recognise that particular species of common bird, such as herring and lesser black backed gulls, can cause nuisance. The law allows measures to be taken against such birds in limited circumstances. 

Action can only be taken under general licences made under the 1981 Act for the purpose of preserving public health or public/air safety, preventing the spread of disease or for preventing serious damage to livestock, foodstuffs for livestock, crops. 

An offence would be committed if action could not be justified or defended for any of these purposes. 

Only the owner or occupier can take action against gulls nesting on their buildings. Alternatively they can give someone else permission to act on their behalf. Any action taken must be justified in terms of the law and can include:

  • The killing or taking of gulls
  • The damaging or destruction of their nests
  • The taking or destruction of their eggs

Action taken must be humane. Any inhumane method which could cause suffering would be illegal. The use of poisons or drugs to take or kill any bird is specifically prohibited except under very special circumstances and with a government licence.

In practice, there are very few humane methods to kill birds which are likely only to affect that particular species and skill and experience is needed to deploy them. The council is particularly keen to discourage shooting of gulls.

If you want to feed birds without encouraging seagulls:

  • Only put out bird food from November to May, as there is nearly always enough natural food available the rest of the year
  • Put food in wire baskets or under a roofed bird table so that only small birds can get it
  • Put out small amounts of food frequently rather than large amounts at a time

Specialist companies can protect your building from gulls by installing wires or spikes on chimney pots and other rooftop features.

Current Council Policy on Seagulls

Although we have no statutory duty to take action against seagulls, since 2007, the council put in place a stakeholder forum to annually review the situation with nesting gulls in Hereford and agree a way forward on their control. This forum includes the public, Herefordshire Council members, Hereford City members, as well as businesses and experts in the control of birds. This forum annually reviews the effectiveness of control measures deployed and considers the various measures for controlling gulls available within the constraints of a government licence, the main ones being egg removal, nest removal, proofing and education.

In 2008, we first entered into a one year contract with Falconry Pest Control Services, who were commissioned to remove gull nests and eggs from April to August in Hereford city only. At this time it was estimated, via an independent survey, that there were between 400 to 500 breeding pairs on Hereford's rooftops.

Since 2009 we have renewed this contract every three years and since 2012 Hereford City Council had agreed to co-fund this work.

Currently, the egg and nest removal service will operate from 2015 until 2018, running from April to August every year, at two-weekly intervals by two contractors working in a van with a lifting platform. At present, this service is free to all, provided that the roof is accessible, roof permission is granted in writing, and the work can be done in under one hour.

The current thinking is that the breeding pairs in Hereford have dropped considerably from 500 in 2008 to approximately 200 in 2015. Taking into consideration an estimated population growth rate of 5% to 20% a year, this has reinforced our belief that the programme is working.

Please note we cannot touch live birds.

Prevention Methods

You can help prevent pest activity on your property by:               

  • Removing any possible food sources including fallen fruit, animal feed and accessible household waste
  • Keeping your rubbish in bins until the day of collection wherever practical
  • Making sure your bins have secured lids
  • Using special bird feeders rather than putting food on the floor
  • Not feeding your dogs and cats outdoors
  • Picking up and disposing of any litter outside your property
  • Clearing any undergrowth from your garden to remove possible nesting places.
  • Not putting any meats, bread or cooked food on a compost heap as this will attract vermin into your garden. Where possible secure fine grade wire meshing around and underneath your compost heap.
  • Commercial properties should keep the streets at the front and back of the premise free of litter and black rubbish bags

Breeding Birds

The usual life cycle of a seagull is 15 to 20 years and they can start breeding after reaching maturity at the age of 4. Breeding birds mate for life and females only have a certain number of eggs they can lay in their lifetime.

Most gulls breed once a year and have predictable breeding seasons lasting for three to six months. The birds lay 2 to 3 speckled eggs in nests composed of vegetation. The incubation period lasts between 22 and 26 days, and begins after laying the first egg. Seagulls are attentive, caring parents and take turns incubating the eggs, and feeding and protecting the chicks.

Controlling Seagull Methods

There have been a few methods to try and cull seagull populations throughout the UK.

One method tested is when the eggs are pricked. This allows air into the protective sack that contains the embryo and stops it from growing further. Eventually, the parents realise the egg is dead and discard it, leaving space for a new egg to be laid.

However, leaving the eggs in the nest in this manner encourages aggressive behaviour from the parents as they remain protective until they discard them.

Another method tested is when the eggs are replaced with fake rubber eggs. However, the same problem occurs as the parents quickly discover the eggs are no longer alive and discard them.

Again, leaving the eggs in the nest in this manner encourages aggressive behaviour from the parents as they remain protective until they discard them.

Another method is when the eggs are oiled. The oil seals the egg so no oxygen can reach the embryo inside. However, as the parents lay on the eggs to incubate them, the oil tends to rub off onto the parent’s feathers and is then cleaned off when the birds preen.

A further method is proofing buildings with nets and / or spikes. However, this is an extremely expensive method and due to the scale of buildings and properties in affected areas, the price in many cases is deemed to not be cost-effective.

The last method tested is when the eggs and nests are removed from a site and this is the method Herefordshire Council have opted to use.

When the eggs have been removed from the nest, seagulls can quickly relay more to replace the ones they have lost. The incubation period lasts between 22 and 26 days, so the eggs are removed every 14 days, long before the chick can hatch. As there are no eggs left in the nest after each visit, the parents lose their aggressive behaviour as there is nothing for them to be protective over.

This is a time consuming method, however, the more eggs removed from the nests, the more eggs a female will lay during a breeding season and will eventually become infertile. 

Contract pest control services

The contract service helps to maintain a pest-free environment. It helps you to conform to legislation and codes of practice, reduce damage caused by pests, and reduce the risk of disease.

We will make a free initial site visit to assess your situation, followed by a written quotation setting out the scope of the contract. This can be tailored to suit your requirements.

We provide full documentation for each contract, including site plans, worksheets and bait schedules, and health and safety data sheets.

There are four levels of services available:

Bronze

This option allows you to receive:

  • 4 planned visits (1 visit every 3 months) as well as 4 unplanned visits to be used at the technician’s discretion
  • One free callout visit for rodents
  • One free callout treatment for insects
  • A total of 10 visits throughout the year

Silver

This option allows you to receive:

  • 6 planned visits (1 visit every 2 months) as well as 6 unplanned visits to be used at the technician’s discretion
  • One free callout visit for rodents
  • One free callout treatment for insects
  • A total of 14 visits throughout the year

Gold

This option allows you to receive:

  • 8 planned visits (1 visit every 6 weeks) as well as 8 unplanned visits to be used at the technician’s discretion
  • One free callout visit for rodents
  • One free callout treatment for insects
  • A total of 18 visits throughout the year

Platinum

This option allows you to receive:

  • 12 planned visits (1 visit every month) as well as 12 unplanned visits to be used at the technician’s discretion
  • One free callout visit for rodents
  • One free callout treatment for insects
  • A total of 26 visits throughout the year
