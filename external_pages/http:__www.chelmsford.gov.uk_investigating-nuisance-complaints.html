Investigating nuisance complaints

    Chelmsford City Council will investigate complaints of nuisance. Nuisance may arise from the following:

     

    • noise from domestic and business premises
    • light from domestic and commercial premises
    • smoke from domestic and commercial premises
    • odour from commercial premises
    • flies from commercial premises
    • dust from commercial premises
    • fumes or gases from domestic premises

     

    If any of the above are unreasonable, we recommend you speak to the person / business responsible first. They may not be aware they are causing a problem and may be able to resolve the matter without difficulty.

     

    The process

     

    If this approach has failed or you feel uncomfortable approaching the business or individual responsible, then contact us. The Environmental Protection Team can investigate nuisance complaints under the Environmental Protection Act 1990 to determine whether or not a Statutory Nuisance exists.

     

    When a complaint is made to the Council, an Officer will send letters to both you and the person responsible for the alleged nuisance. This initial contact will hopefully resolve the issue.

     

    If the nuisance persists, you will be asked to keep a log, normally over 14 days. You should record what you can smell, see, hear etc. and for how long and at what time of day. We will then use this information to see if there is any pattern to the problem and then try to find the cause and resolve the issue. The log sheets will show if the nuisance is likely to amount to a Statutory Nuisance. They will also show the times when an Officer is most likely to witness the nuisance. 

     

    If the nuisance is statutory, we will serve an abatement notice requiring the person responsible to take remedial action.

     

    In rare cases where the notice is not complied with and the problem still persists, legal proceedings may follow. Please noted that commercial and industrial premises have a legal defence known as 'Best Practicable Means' and it may not be possible to stop a nuisance arising from such premises completely. However, we can control it to an acceptable level.

     

    This procedure can take time but it is designed to be fair to everyone concerned.

     

    Statutory nuisance relies on the concept of the average person i.e. does the nuisance interfere with the “average” person’s reasonable enjoyment of their property. In other words, an unpleasant odour or a noise in someone's garden in the winter that does not enter their house or is not audible inside the house would not constitute a statutory nuisance. This is because the "average" person would not be expected to spend significant periods of time in their garden during cold weather. However, during warmer weather they are more likely to be in the garden and therefore the same odour or noise would be more likely to constitute a statutory nuisance.

     

    Confidentiality

    We will keep details of anyone making a complaint confidential and the general public will not have access to names and addresses. However, it may be necessary to reveal the identity of a complainant to support the Council's case if formal legal action is necessary. In these cases the Council will give the prior notification before the case gets to this stage.
