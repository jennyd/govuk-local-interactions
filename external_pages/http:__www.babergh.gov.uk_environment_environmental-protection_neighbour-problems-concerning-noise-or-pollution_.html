Neighbour Problems Concerning Noise or Pollution

What is a Statutory Nuisance?

The Council can help to deal with pollution that amounts to a ‘statutory nuisance’ under the Environmental Protection Act 1990. To be a statutory nuisance something must be either prejudicial to health or a nuisance in common law terms. What constitutes a statutory nuisance is not easily defined. However, in general terms, it is something that interferes unreasonably with your enjoyment of your property, a regular disturbance that continues sufficiently to be deemed 'unreasonable'. A statutory nuisance will be more than 'annoyance' and much more than the mere detection of pollution or a problem. Many other factors determine whether or not a something amounts to a statutory nuisance including how often it occurs, how long it lasts for, the time of day and your location (e.g. urban/rural).    

What things are classified as Statutory Nuisances?

Issues which may be controlled using statutory nuisance law include:

  • Noise
  • Smoke
  • Accumulations
  • Animals kept in such a place or manner as to be prejudicial to health or a nuisance
  • Dust and steam (commercial premises only)
  • Fumes and gases (applies to domestic premises only)
  • Insects (from relevant industrial, trade or business premises)
  • Light (from badly adjusted floodlights, for example)
  • Odour (commercial premises only)
  • Premises   

What things are not classified as Statutory Nuisances?

Nuisance is a property based issue so, for example, an issue that disturbed you whilst walking on a public footpath could not be a statutory nuisance to you.  Other issues that are not classified as statutory nuisances include;

  • Aircraft noise
  • Domestic odours (like cooking smells)
  • Normal road noise

In such cases, whilst we will not be able to take formal action we may be able to give you advice or suggest your best course of action.  

What can you do about the problem?

In the first instance, talk or write to the person or business responsible to explain politely that you are being troubled and the difficulties they are causing. You could use our Template Letters as guidance. Remember to keep a copy.  You may find this difficult, but often people are unaware that they are causing a problem. Most will be glad to do what they can to prevent or minimise the problem - a reasonable approach may encourage a reasonable response. Approach the matter carefully. If you think your neighbour might react angrily to a complaint then take a mutual acquaintance or a friend with you. If this approach fails the Council may be able to help.

You can make a complaint to the Council using the contact details opposite or use our online Report It form.  

How are complaints dealt with?

Before we can take legal action on anyone's behalf, we must be satisfied that a statutory nuisance exists and that evidence (suitable for a Court) has been gathered to support the case. We will make a professional judgment about whether the problem fits within the terms of statutory nuisance legislation. Complaints are confidential, although we cannot prevent the source from making guesses about the identity of the complainant. Also, if a case proceeds to prosecution then you would be required to support the case with a witness statement, and you would probably be required to give evidence to the court.

  • An informal letter will be sent, where appropriate, to the person or business complained of to inform them of the problem. This can usually resolve complaints without any further action being taken.
  • You may be asked to keep an accurate diary, detailing when the problem occurs and how the problem affects you. It is important that the diary is returned.  If it is not, we will assume that the problem has been resolved and the case will be closed.
  • Noise monitoring equipment may be installed in your premises if the diary indicates a nuisance may exist. 
  • An officer of the Council may visit when the problem occurs to make an assessment.
  • If a statutory nuisance is found to exist, an 'Abatement Notice' will be served to prohibit or restrict it in the future.
  • If the problem continues, you will be asked to maintain a nuisance diary and an officer will make further visits when the problem is alleged to occur.
  • The final option is prosecute the 'offender' in the Magistrates’ Court where a fine may be imposed or equipment forfeited.   

Taking your own action

If you do not wish to involve the Council, or if we do not feel it is appropriate to take action, then you can pursue your own nuisance action by contacting the Magistrates’ Court directly. This is quite simple and need not cost much; you do not need to employ a solicitor, but it is advisable to obtain some legal advice.  If you contact us using the details below we can send you a booklet explaining the process and giving further guidance.
