The roads we salt

You can download our salting maps for Central Bedfordshire (PDF 5.5MB), Dunstable (PDF 747KB)  and Leighton Buzzard (PDF 980KB).

Our salting network (PDF 223KB) has been created so that roads carrying the highest amount of traffic, the highest risk of traffic and the highest risk of accidents are treated.

We salt just under half of the road network in Central Bedfordshire. We try to ensure that the majority of road users live within a reasonable distance of a salted road. In the event of snow, roads will be cleared according to their priority.

A review in 2011 redefined the priority 0 routes that comes into operation if required by the government to reduce salt usage. The main changes implemented were to include important link roads (to upper and middle schools, ambulance / fire station within this network).

Priorty 0 roads

This is a network that is treated in the rare event that resources are not available for treating the highway authorities priority 1 networks.

A and B class carriageways, plus certain other roads serving the upper and middle schools, and the premises of the emergency services that are not on or very close to A and B roads.

Priority 1 roads

The priority 1 network (PDF 223KB) is the network of roads that is routinely treated for ice and snow. Priority will be given to maintaining the priority 1 network clear of ice and snow.

The priority 1 network is defined as:

  • all A and B class roads
  • most C class roads 
  • some UC class roads

It includes:

  • busy peak commuter routes
  • main peak hour bus routes
  • routes to fire stations
  • ambulance stations
  • hospitals
  • most but not all school bus routes
  • roads that's past all middle and upper schools

Priority 2 roads

This is the road network that, although not treated as a regular priority, is considered important enough to warrant treatment during prolonged winter weather when the priority 1 network is passable by traffic, free from major ice and snow, and resources are available to add this priority 2 network (PDF 223KB) to the priority 1 salting and or ploughing regime.

If you have any questions, please telephone the Highway Helpdesk on 0300 300 8409. Bedfordshire Highways do not look after motorways and trunk roads. Carillion URS look after the A421. They can be contacted on 01536 413700.

Local gritting by Town and Parish Councils

Some Town and Parish Councils provide a local gritting service to cover non priority roads and car parks which are not covered by Central Bedfordshire Council. Such schemes can only be undertaken with the delegated authority of Central Bedfordshire Council and are delivered according to their own local Statement of Purpose.

You can contact your Town / Parish Council to check if your area is covered.

When we salt

Salting is carried out when road temperatures drop below zero and when the roads are expected to be damp. Ice will not form on a dry road surface in dry air. We try to carry out salting either after the evening or before the morning peak traffic periods. We may work 24 hours around the clock if we have bad weather.

We use a computer system that collects weather and road information at different sites across Central Bedfordshire. The Met Office uses this weather information along with their other weather data to predict weather conditions and road surface temperatures. We then make our decision on whether or not to salt and at what time of the day or night.

Salt bins - locations, refills and getting a new one

There are over 200 salt bins (PDF 149KB) set out on the public highway across Central Bedfordshire. The salt bins (PDF 149KB) are provided for the public to use on the roads and footways and are generally sited at bends or hills where the risk of slipping on ice is greater.

Salt bins (PDF 149KB) are not provided for people to use within their own private property. If you wish to do this, most supermarkets and builders merchants hold good stocks of de-icing salt for you to buy.

If you are a resident of Central Bedfordshire you can use this new account to request salt bins are filled or request a new salt bin. You can make the following reports about salting and gritting:

  • report damage from a gritting lorry
  • request your road is added to the salting route
  • request a snow plough

You can also tell us about:

  • roads / carriageways e.g. damaged road surfaces, obstructions and spillages
  • drainage e.g. blocked gullies, flooding and blocked ditches
  • footpaths e.g. damaged pavements, trip hazards and loose paving slabs
  • trees e.g. overgrown trees, fallen branches or trees blocking light

The Snow Code - tips on clearing snow and ice

The Snow Code

The Snow Code advice issued by the Department for Transport on clearing snow and ice from the pavement outside your home or public spaces to prevent slips and falls. Follow the snow code to clear snow and ice safely. The snow code contains tips on clearing snow and ice from pavements or public spaces.

Don't be put off clearing paths because you're afraid someone will get injured. Remember, people walking on snow and ice have a responsibility to be careful themselves.

Follow the advice below to make sure you clear the pathway safely and effectively.

And don't believe the myths - it's unlikely you'll be sued or held legally responsible for any injuries if you have cleared the path carefully.

Clear the snow and ice early in the day


It's easier to move fresh, loose snow rather than hard snow that has packed together from people walking on it. So if possible, start removing the snow and ice in the morning. If you remove the top layer of snow in the morning, any sunshine during the day will help melt any ice beneath. You can then cover the path with salt before nightfall to stop it refreezing overnight.

Preventing slips

Pay extra attention to clearing snow and ice from steps and steep pathways - you might need to use more salt on these areas.Use salt or sand - not water.

Don't make the pathways more dangerous by causing them to refreeze. If you use water to melt the snow, it may refreeze and turn to black ice. Black ice increases the risk of injuries as it is invisible and very slippery. 

You can melt snow or prevent black ice by spreading some salt on the area you have cleared. You can use ordinary table or dishwasher salt - a tablespoon for each square metre you clear should work. Don't use the salt found in salting bins - this will be needed to keep the roads clear. 

Be careful not to spread salt on plants or grass as it may damage them. 

If you don't have enough salt, you can also use sand or ash. These won't stop the path icing over as effectively as salt, but will provide good grip underfoot.Take care where you move the snow. When you're shovelling snow, take care where you put it so it doesn't block people's paths or drains. Make sure you make a path down the middle of the area to be cleared first, so you have a clear surface to walk on. Then shovel the snow from the centre of the path to the sides.

Offer to clear your neighbours' paths

If your neighbour will have difficulty getting in and out of their home, offer to clear snow and ice around their property as well. Check that any elderly or disabled neighbours are alright in the cold weather. If you're worried about them, try contacting their relatives or friends, or if necessary contact us.

Salt bag scheme - Town and Parish Councils

We will deliver either one 1 tonne or one ½ tonne bag of road de-icing salt, free of charge, to any town or parish council that requests one. We will do this in December, January and February if requested by the Town / Parish  Council.

This scheme gives town and parish councils the opportunity to take responsibility for bags and identify local community volunteers to help spread the salt on minor roads and pavements that are not covered by our gritting routes.

Each bag will need to be signed for and kept on private land.

Volunteer led Winter Salting Groups

We encourage local communities, with the help of their town / parish council to create Winter Salting Groups, ideally as part of a local Community Emergency Response Team (CERT). Despite the misconception of liability, reassuringly, in Central Bedfordshire, if volunteers follow the advice below and  clear snow in a safe and sensible manner as part of a co-ordinated volunteer scheme, they will be covered under Bedfordshire and Luton Emergency Volunteers Executive Partnership (BLEVEP) policy provided by Zurich. To qualify under this scheme, volunteers must have registered with the BLEVEP Partnership, have read the Government’s snow code and follow the Volunteer Health and Safety brief (PDF 18KB).

Town and Parish Councils can also help volunteers to identify salt storage points and how volunteers can access salting facilities and equipment. 

Setting Up a Winter Salting Group

First find out if your area / community already has a team in place that you can join. See the Bedfordshire and Luton Local Resilience Forum website for a current list of Community Emergency Response Teams and their contact details.

A team of volunteers contact their Town or Parish Council and register interest in setting up a Winter Salting Group (ideally as part of a Community Emergency Response Team (CERT). 

Volunteers work out a list of vulnerable areas for priority salting and confirm with Town / Parish Council and Community Emergency Response Team. Schools are a priority to keep open, volunteers should work with the Head Teacher and Site Agent.

Volunteers check location of salt bins and priority areas for local salting. Recruit more volunteers using notice boards, parish magazines, approaching local clubs and organisations. Volunteers read and sign up to the Volunteer Health and Safety brief (PDF 18KB). Volunteers complete the registration form (PDF 4KB) and send to the Bedfordshire and Luton Emergency Volunteers Partnership to qualify for cover under the BLEVEC Insurance for Emergency volunteers.
