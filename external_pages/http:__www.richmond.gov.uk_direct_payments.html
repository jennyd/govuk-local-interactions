Direct Payments

A Direct Payment is an amount of money we give you to help you live independently in your own home. You use the money to buy and arrange the care and support you need.

Receiving a Direct Payment does not affect any other benefits you may already get.

Related topic

  • Direct Payment Prepaid Card

Who can get a Direct Payment?

We will tell you if you are eligible for this type of support once you have had an assessment.

More about assessments

Managing your money

If you are able to manage your payments yourself you can get advice on how to do this by the team who assessed you.

If you are unable to manage them yourself you can ask someone else to do it for you. This will need to be agreed by the team who assessed you.

Hestia Support offers a support service for people receiving Direct Payments.

If you care for someone

If you care for someone who is unable to manage the money themselves and you have Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA), you may be able to receive a Direct Payment on their behalf.

If you don’t have LPA you may still be able to receive a Direct Payment on behalf of the person you care for. You will need to discuss this with the assessment team.

Receiving your payments

Payments are made monthly. The amount paid each month will vary depending on the number of days in that particular month.

The first payment will be calculated either from:

  • the date your Support Plan was agreed; or
  • the date a signed Direct Payment Agreement was received.

We will write to you to tell you when the first payment will be made.

We can pay your Direct Payment directly into a Direct Payment Prepaid Card account, which means you would not have to set up a separate bank account.

More on Prepaid Cards

Using your Direct Payments

The support you buy with your Direct Payment must be legal and must meet the outcomes that you have listed in your Support Plan. You can use your Direct Payment in a flexible way to meet those outcomes.

You can employ your own care worker (sometimes called a Personal Assistant), or you can use a care agency.

You could also use your Direct Payment to make other purchases, such as:

  • a small piece of equipment that will support your independence;
  • a short break away from home to give your carer a break; or
  • an activity or class you enjoy, instead of going to a day centre.

If you are not sure what you can use your Direct Payment for, contact the team which assessed you before you spend any money.

You cannot spend your Direct Payment on:

  • anything not related to your care and support;
  • residential or nursing care (excluding respite - for periods of up to 4 consecutive weeks residential care in any 12 month period) ;
  • anything illegal (including paying people cash in hand);
  • employing family members who already live with you;
  • household bills, clothes, food, paying off debt;
  • services which should be provided by the NHS;
  • services normally funded through other benefits or schemes (for example support into employment);
  • major daily living equipment or adaptations to your house;
  • anything that places you at risk; or
  • things that do not represent value for money (for example when there is a valid cheaper alternative available).

Your responsibilities

While Direct Payments offer greater choice and control over how you meet your care and support needs, they also carry responsibilities. You (or the person you choose to help you manage your money) will be responsible for making sure that the services you are buying with your Direct Payment are contributing to the outcomes agreed in your support plan and that you are complying with all legal requirements.

Many people use Direct Payments to employ their own Personal Assistants. This option gives you the most direct control, but it also carries many additional responsibilities. For example, you must pay their tax, national insurance contributions, statutory sick pay, maternity pay and redundancy.

You can read more about employment law at GOV.UK.

You may choose to have a contract with independent agencies or with self-employed individuals, instead of employing a Personal Assistant.

When your circumstances change

If your care or support needs change, you should discuss this with your social worker. In some cases this will mean that we will need to make a change to your direct payments. We will always discuss the details with you and explain these changes in writing.

Repayments

If we find that your Direct Payment has not been used to meet the outcomes agreed in your Support Plan, you may be required to repay some or all of your Direct Payment. We will always discuss the details with you and work with you to agree how you should repay the money.

If there is money left over in your account which is more than eight times your weekly allocation, you may need to make a repayment. We will make sure you have enough money left to pay for your support until you receive your next payment.
