In the first instance this is requested however, the powers delegated under legislation allow notices to be served. If the works are not carried out, we can consider doing the works in default and charging the property concerned, as a land charge.

Types of Drainage

There are various types of drainage system:
 
Septic Tanks and Soakaway
Cesspools
Treatment Plants
Private/Public or Mains Drains

Septic tanks and Soakaways

Septic tank systems comprise of two parts. The tank provides suitable conditions for gravity settlement, where bacteria can digest and partially decompose the solids. There should be a scum layer on the top helping these bacteria to have the correct conditions to live. The liquid element is passed on to a perforated piped system to 'soakaway'. The soil flora and fauna then digest this liquid and cleanse it.

The maintenance of these systems is important to ensure that they function normally. A septic tank should be de-sludged (removal of the solids) at least once a year to prevent clogging of the soakaway. In this district, it is normal to empty the tank at least once a quarter, however in areas of a high water table or poor drainage and high rainfall this can be significantly increased in the winter months.

The drainage field or soakaway can become blocked or damaged overtime and usually last no more than 10 to 15 years.
 
Indicators of faulty septic tanks system are:
  • slowly draining sinks, toilets, baths etc
  • covers lifted by overflowing solids
  • sewage building up
  • land surface flooding
  • ground movement near the tank system
  • pollution of nearby watercourse or ditches
  • general odour of sewage
Note: You are not allowed to have a direct connection to a watercourse or ditch. Should you need to rebuild or re-site your soakaway, you should ensure that there is at least 6 metres from the soakaway edge to the nearest watercourse.

What you can do to prevent problems from occurring;
  • Inspect the system once a month, get to know your system, you are then more likely to recognise problems when they occur and prior to any major works being required.
  • De-sludge at least once a year
  • Act if problems are found
  • Ensure air vents are not blocked
  • Protect your soakaway from plants or drivers!
  • Do provide separate soakaways for rainwater pipes and ensure that these do not flood the drainage field, as it will inhibit the effectiveness of the soakaway system.
  • Don't dispose of nappies or sanitary items, plastic or solids, as they will cause blockages.
  • Don't pour paints, solvents, fats, grease, pesticides or hazardous chemicals down the drains, not only will this kill off the bacteria but it may also dissolve your pipes!
  • Don't empty chemical toilets into it.
  • Don't ever enter septic tank, a build up of toxic gases can occur as part of the natural treatment process.
Cesspools

These are enclosed tanks, which will require emptying once full. There is no soakaway attachment and no additional parts. It is ideal for use in areas where the ground water is close to ground level or where the soil does not have good drainage.

Treatment Plant

A treatment plant can be built for an individual property or many. They are an alternative to the septic tank system and require an electronic supply. The same 'do's & don'ts' apply as the septic tank system. A treatment plant will require regular de-sludging and maintenance. There will be a primary chamber acting similar to the septic tank and a secondary chamber where treatment occurs, this can be chemical or biological. The cleansed effluent is then clean enough to discharge to a water course, where a 'consent to discharge' has been agreed by the Environment Agency.

Private/Public and Mains Drains

A drain or public sewer is the responsibility of the users to maintain unless the public sewer is built before 1937, then the sewage undertaker has this responsibility. All mains drainage is the responsibility of the water authority for that district, here that is Anglian Water Services.
