licensing act 2003 licences

Listen

The Licensing Act 2003 came into force in November 2005. It established a single regime for licensing premises used for sale or supply of alcohol, the provision of regulated entertainment or late night refreshment.

Licensable activities are

  • The sale by retail of alcohol
  • For qualifying clubs, the supply of alcohol to a club member or guest
  • The provision of regulated entertainment - films, live music, recorded music, indoor sport events, plays, performances of dance
  • The provision of late night refreshment - selling hot food or drink between 11pm and 5am

These activities can be authorised by one of the following:

  • Premises Licence - authorises activities on a permanent basis
  • Club Premises Certificate -  authorises activities on a permanent basis for qualifying clubs
  • Temporary Event Notice - for small scale one off events

The act also established personal licences. A personal licence allows an individual to sell alcohol or authorise the sale of alcohol under the authority of a premises licence.  Premises licences authorising the sale of alcohol are required to have a Designated Premises Supervisor specified on the licence (there is an exemption for community premises). This individual must hold a personal licence and is responsible for authorising sales of alcohol at the premises. This person must be readily available and is usually responsible for the day to day running of the premises.

what to do next...

  • Download, fill in and return the appropriate form(s) to the Licensing Team with the relevant fees as detailed on the fees and charges page
  • Search the Licensing Registers online
    • As the licensing authority for the Licensing Act 2003, we are required to maintain a register of all applications received, and licenses issued, under the Act (Premises Licences, Club Premises Certificates, Personal Licences and Temporary Event Notices)
  • Complain about a premises or activity in Broadland covered by the Licensing Act 2003 (see further information below).
  • Contact the Licensing Team for additional information and advice

further information

The Licensing Act 2003 provides a clear focus on the promotion of four statutory objectives which must be addressed when licensing functions are undertaken.

The licensing objectives are:

  • The prevention of crime and disorder
  • Public safety
  • The prevention of public nuisance; and
  • The protection of children from harm

The Licensing Act requires a Licensing Authority to determine and publish a statement of licensing policy once every five years.
