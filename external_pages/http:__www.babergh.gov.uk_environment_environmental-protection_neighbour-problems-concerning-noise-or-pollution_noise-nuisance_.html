Noise Nuisance

What is a Noise Nuisance?

The Council can help to deal with noise that amounts to a ‘statutory nuisance’ under the Environmental Protection Act 1990. To be a statutory nuisance the noise must be either prejudicial to health or a nuisance in common law terms. What constitutes a statutory nuisance is not easily defined. However, in general terms, it is something that interferes unreasonably with your enjoyment of your property, a regular disturbance that continues sufficiently to be deemed 'unreasonable'.

A statutory nuisance will be more than 'annoyance' and much more than the mere detection of a noise. Many other factors determine whether or not a noise is a statutory nuisance including how often it occurs, the time of day, your location (e.g. urban/rural) and the volume, character and duration of the noise. There are no fixed levels of noise which constitute a statutory nuisance and no set times within which it is acceptable to carry out noisy activities. 

Types of noise which may be a Statutory Nuisance include:

  • Noise from domestic properties which becomes persistent and/or intrusive, e.g. music, barking dogs
  • Noise from burglar alarms
  • Noise from industrial, commercial, agricultural or business premises, e.g. machinery and equipment
  • Noise from construction sites, e.g. building operations
  • Noise from car alarms and loudspeakers in the street

We cannot take action against:

  • Aircraft noise
  • Road traffic noise
  • Noisy vehicles on the highway (horns, revving, exhausts)
  • One-off parties
  • Noise caused by people walking or congregating in the street - but if this is a persistent problem, we may be able to assist, see our page about antisocial behaviour
  • General everyday living noises such as dogs barking occasionally, lawnmowers used during the day, babies crying, children playing, slamming doors, walking upstairs and raised voices. These issues are best dealt with by mediation and you may find the following websites helpful:
    • Catch22 Mediation Service
    • Citizens Advice Bureau
    • Directgov.uk

What can you do about the problem?

In the first instance, talk or write to the person or business responsible to explain politely that you are being troubled by noise and the difficulties they are causing. You could use our Template Letters as guidance. Remember to keep a copy.

You may find this difficult, but often people are unaware that they are causing a problem. Most will be glad to do what they can to prevent or minimise the noise - a reasonable approach may encourage a reasonable response.

Approach the matter carefully. If you think your neighbour might react angrily to a complaint then take a mutual acquaintance or a friend with you. If this approach fails the Council may be able to help.

You can make a complaint to the Council using the contact details opposite or our online Report It form.

How are complaints dealt with?

Before we can take legal action on anyone's behalf, we must be satisfied that a statutory nuisance exists and that evidence (suitable for a Court) has been gathered to support the case. We will make a professional judgment about whether the problem fits within the terms of statutory nuisance legislation. Complaints are confidential although we cannot prevent the source from making guesses about the identity of the complainant. Also, if a case proceeds to prosecution then you would be required to support the case with a witness statement, and you would probably be required to give evidence to the court.

  • An informal letter will be sent, where appropriate, to the person or business complained of to inform them of the problem. This can usually resolve complaints without any further action being taken.
  • You will be asked to keep an accurate diary, detailing when the noise occurs and how the noise affects you. It is important that the diary is returned within 4 weeks. If it is not, we will assume that the problem has been resolved and the case will be closed.
  • Noise monitoring equipment may be installed in your premises if the diary indicates a nuisance exists.
  • An officer of the Council may visit when the noise occurs to make an assessment.
  • If a statutory nuisance is found to exist, an 'Abatement Notice' will be served to prohibit or restrict the noise.
  • If the noise continues, you will be asked to maintain a nuisance diary and an officer will make further visits when the noise is alleged to occur.
  • In some cases the Council may obtain a warrant to enter premises and seize noise-making equipment.
  • The final option is prosecute the 'offender' in the Magistrates’ Court where a fine may be imposed and their equipment forfeited.

Taking your own action

If you do not wish to involve the Council, or if we do not feel it is appropriate to take action, then you can pursue your own action by contacting the Magistrates’ Court directly. This is quite simple and need not cost much; you do not need to employ a solicitor, but it is advisable to obtain some legal advice. If you contact us using the details below we can send you a booklet explaining the process and giving further guidance.

Who else is concerned with controlling noise?

  • Suffolk Police - Motor vehicles on public highways (horns, revving, exhausts)
  • Ministry of Defence - Military aircraft
  • British Airports Authority - Civil aircraft (take-off and landing), contact the airport concerned via the BAA
  • Civil Aviation Authority – Civil aircraft in flight, light aircraft and micro-lights
