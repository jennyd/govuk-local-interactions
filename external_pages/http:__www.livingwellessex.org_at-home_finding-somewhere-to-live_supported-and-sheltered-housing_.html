Supported and sheltered housing

You may feel that you've reached a phase when living in your current home is becoming too difficult.

Perhaps you're struggling to get around it. Maybe you're no longer able to do everything for yourself. Or maybe you're just feeling a bit isolated and lonely.

At the same time you may not want to give up your independence and privacy, and don't need as much assistance as is offered in a care home or extra care housing. If this is the case, sheltered housing may be the solution for you.

Sheltered and supported housing schemes (sometimes known as retirement housing) are properties for rent aimed at people over 60 years of age, although depending on where you live some schemes are available for slightly younger people.

There is no upper age limit on who can live there. Tenants are generally able to do most things for themselves. They're often still quite active, want to be as independent as possible and come and go as they please.

And if a tenant does need some assistance with day-to-day tasks then they can arrange this as they would in any other accommodation.

Sheltered and supported housing properties are usually self-contained, unfurnished, purpose-built flats, houses or bungalows with their own front doors, kitchens and bathrooms.

They're usually designed to be easily accessible for people with wheelchairs or other mobility issues. Plus, many schemes will have flats for couples, as well as one-bedroom and studio flats for single people.

There's often also a communal area where residents can socialise and organise and attend events and activities, and there'll usually be a communal laundry and gardens.

What is sheltered accommodation?

In sheltered accommodation there's usually a property manager or scheme manager who lives on site.

These managers will often visit or call residents to make sure that they're well and comfortable. They'll address any concerns residents may have and offer advice. And they can put them in touch with additional support when required.

Residents will also be provided with an alarm system so that they can call for help in an emergency at any time of the day or night. And it's the scheme manager who'll be the person who usually responds to an alarm call.

What is Extra Care Housing?

Some parts of Essex have 'Extra Care' schemes. These are often owned by Housing Associations or charitable trusts.

Extra care tenants have their own self-contained accommodation and there are usually communal facilites over and above what might be found in a sheltered scheme, e.g. restaurant, hairdresser, health suite.

Most importantly, there will be a twenty-four hour on-site care provision available for people with additional needs.

These schemes are suitable for those with existing care needs, or for people who are becoming frailer and want the security of knowing they'll not need to move into residential care.

Details of Extra Care schemes are available from your local Housing Department or Social Services.

Paying for sheltered accommodation

If you move to a specialist sheltered or supported accommodation scheme you'll have to pay rent on your home, and sometimes an additional service charge to cover the services of the scheme manager, as well as general maintenance.

If you're eligible for Housing Benefit then you'll be able to claim this towards the cost of your rent.

For some people on qualifying benefits, Essex County Council will contribute to the support costs through the 'Housing Related Support' fund.

How to apply for sheltered and supported accommodation

Sheltered schemes are usually owned by your local council, or run by a housing association on behalf of the council. In Essex, there are also a number of other providers, especially almshouses.

Sheltered scheme tenants will have a secure tenancy with the same rights as other council or Housing Association tenants.

You'll usually have to complete the same application form as you would for any council housing, but should indicate that you're particularly interested in sheltered accommodation.

You may then be visited by someone from the housing department to gather more information. They'll ask you questions about your health, mobility, family situation and your finances, so that they can check if you're eligible for supported or sheltered accommodation and find a property that suits your needs.

Your local council provides more information on how sheltered housing work in your area.

Private sheltered housing

There are also a number of private providers of supported and sheltered accommodation. These properties are usually purchased rather than rented.  You can search for available accommodation on the Housing Care website.

You'll find that many high street estate agents will also advertise supported and sheltered accommodation as it become available.

And the following property websites will often advertise private, supported and sheltered schemes which are for sale: 

  • Right Move
  • Zoopla
  • Prime Location

Other information and advice

Age UK provide independent advice on accessing sheltered housing schemes, and on your other housing options in older age.

NHS Carers Direct has advice and information for people considering a move to sheltered accommodation, either for themselves or for a relative. 

The Housing Care website has advice on sheltered accommodation.
