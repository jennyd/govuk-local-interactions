Anti-social behaviour

Last updated: 08/01/2016

Information for Cornwall Housing tenants about anti-social behaviour

One of the biggest threats to a peaceful neighbourhood or estate is anti-social behaviour.

Cornwall Housing has a specialist team which investigates reports of anti-social behaviour and aims to resolve any issues by working alongside the local community and local agencies such as Devon and Cornwall Police.

Examples of anti-social behaviour include: (this is not an exhaustive list)

  • Physical violence against people and property.
  • Domestic violence.
  • Verbal abuse/harassment/intimidation/threatening behaviour.
  • Hate related incidents based on race, nationality, sexual orientation, gender, disability, religion or age.
  • Dealing or supplying drugs, or use of premises for other unlawful purposes.
  • Alcohol or solvent abuse.
  • Noise nuisance (for example: loud parties; shouting; noise from TV or hi-fi equipment; alarms).
  • Criminal damage and vandalism.
  • Rowdy or nuisance behaviour.
  • Misuse of communal areas / public space or loitering.

What can I do about it?

  • If you have a problem with your neighbour, or your neighbour has a problem with you, it is important to try to resolve it as early as possible. Once a problem escalates, it can result in ongoing conflict that becomes very unpleasant for all concerned and very difficult to resolve.
  • If it feels safe to do so, speak to the neighbour directly.  They may not realise that their actions have been upsetting you.  This approach often resolves the matter, improves neighbour relations and provides a more long term solution. 
  • Listen to your neighbours as they may tell you things that cause them annoyance.
  • Ask yourself if your neighbour has a different lifestyle or culture to you and if their actions really are unreasonable.  Sometimes increasing our own tolerance a little bit can help resolve problems.
  • Discuss the practical aspects of the problem, how it is affecting you both and what needs to be done to solve it.  Be sure to treat your neighbour with courtesy and respect and listen to what they have to say. Keep a record of all contact you have regarding the problem.
  • Be prepared to compromise.  Consider the issues carefully at the outset and work out which issues you can live with ... and which you can’t.  You should be reasonably tolerant and understanding of different lifestyles and the behaviours of others.
  • If contact with your neighbour becomes violent or threatening, call the police.

In an emergency, phone 999. If it’s a crime but not an emergency, you can call the police on 101.

Action the council may take against the tenant causing the problem

  • Issue verbal and written warnings
  • Issue a final warning contract
  • Extension or termination of introductory tenancy
  • Demotion of tenancy
  • Serve a notice to seek possession of their property
  • Apply for a Criminal Behaviour Order (in conjunction with Devon & Cornwall Police)
  • Apply for an Civil Injunction (in conjunction with Devon & Cornwall Police)
  • Enforce an Acceptable Behaviour Contract (in conjunction with Devon & Cornwall Police)
  • Apply for a Closure Order on the property (in conjunction with Devon & Cornwall Police)
  • Seek possession of the property (Eviction)

Your responsibilities

The tenancy agreement which you signed when you took over your home makes you responsible not just for your own actions, but also those of anyone living with you or just visiting - including young children.

This means we can take action against you for the anti-social or criminal behaviour of anyone connected to you which affects your neighbours or people living nearby.

To resolve a case we will 

Cornwall Housing takes a balanced approach to tackling anti-social behaviour and considers the effects it will have on the victims, witnesses and the community. 

We have a team of Enforcement Officers who will try to resolve any problems by using a range of informal and formal methods and take action when appropriate.  Eviction is always the last resort.

The Neighbourhood Enforcement Officer will contact the complainant for full details and issue nuisance diaries.  We will ask you to record details of what happened, where and when, and how long it went on for, providing accurate information about specific and ongoing incidents.  This is very important if evidence is needed at a later stage. 

Regular contact will be kept with the complainant and we will offer support and advice throughout the process, as well as monitoring the situation.

We will contact the person alleged to be causing the anti-social behaviour, making them aware of the nuisance they are causing and asking them to modify their behaviour.  Wherever possible, Cornwall Housing will try to find a solution to resolve the problem without the need to take legal action.

Tools available to us include mediation, so both parties can discuss any issues and find a solution to the problem together. In some instances sound recording equipment may be installed in the community which can provide us with evidence of noise nuisance.  We also work in partnership with the police and other agencies e.g. drug, alcohol, mental health to provide the necessary support to all parties involved.

If persistent anti-social behaviour continues to be a problem, we will need to take further action against the person causing the anti-social behaviour using the tools available to us.  Any enforcement action that we take will be reasonable and proportionate to the nature of the case and the evidence we have.
