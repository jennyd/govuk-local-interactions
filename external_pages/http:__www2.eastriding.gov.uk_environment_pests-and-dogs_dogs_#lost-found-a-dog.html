Can I report a barking dog?

Yes, you can report a barking dog online (opens in new window) or by calling us on (01482) 396301, but we suggest you try an informal approach in the first instance, as involving the council can sometimes damage relations with your neighbours.  

Talking to your neighbour

If you do not feel comfortable speaking to them directly at first, we have provided a template letter which you can amend for your situation.

Initial noise letter for barking dog (word 24kb opens in new window)

Making a complaint

After submitting your complaint, you will be sent a 'noise diary sheet' to complete and return.  Our dog wardens will then endeavour to resolve the complaint without having to take legal action, by giving practical advice and demonstrations.  Legal action may be taken if no improvement is made.

Can I report dog fouling?

If the same person repeatedly lets their dog foul and you know who it is, you can report dog fouling using our online form:

Report dog fouling online

or

Tel: (01482) 396301.  Our dog wardens can then investigate. 

You can also ask the council to clean a street or public place if dog fouling has become a problem:

Report a street or public place that needs cleaning

Read more about Dog waste bins.

Are there laws on dog fouling?

Yes, there are local laws in the East Riding, known as 'dog control orders'.  The following pdf document provides an overview of these laws.

Overview of dog control orders (pdf 524kb opens in new window)

In general, it is an offence not to clear up after your dog if it fouls on land in the open air which the public has access to.  A person guilty of this offence can be issued with a fixed penalty notice of £75.

The following pdf document provides more detail on this law.

What happens if you don't clear up after your dog (pdf 49kb opens in new window)

Urinating

There are no specific laws about dogs urinating in public places.  Dog owners are always encouraged to be responsible, and considerate to other people's property, whenever walking their dog.

What are parish councils doing to tackle dog fouling problems?

The council are working with parish councils to make them aware of how to report offenders to the dog wardens. 

We aim to work closely with those we have trained to help reduce the amount of dog fouling in their area, and invitations will be made for parish councils to join the scheme.

Resources for parish councils

Parish councils can visit the resources for parish councils to tackle dog fouling page for more information on support provided by the council.

Are there laws on where I can walk my dog/using a lead/wearing a collar and tag?

Yes, there are local laws in the East Riding, known as 'dog control orders'.  The following pdf document provides an overview of these laws.

Overview of dog control orders (pdf 524kb opens in new window)

These include certain areas where people are not allowed to take their dogs, such as play parks, school grounds and some beaches during summer.  They also include areas where you must keep your dog on a lead, for example certain cemeteries,  churchyards, and promenades during summer.

We would always ask that dogs are kept under close control when out on walks.  

The laws in more detail

Beaches, play parks and school grounds

A list of beaches, play parks and school grounds where dogs are not allowed (pdf 84kb opens in new window)

Taking your dog to the beach

Dogs are not allowed on some beaches during summer.  Read more about taking your dog to the beach

Use of leads

A list of cemeteries, churchyards and promenades where dogs must be on a lead (pdf 58kb opens in new window)

What happens when you don't put your dog on a lead when asked by an authorised officer (pdf 45kb opens in new window)

Identity requirements

If in a public place or on a highway, dogs must wear a collar with a tag or badge attached, with the name and address of the owner inscribed on it.

How we care for stray dogs

The council will pick up stray dogs from public places where possible, or collect stray dogs that are being held by a member of the public. If the dog has a collar and tag with the owner's name and address, we will return the dog where possible. If the dog is microchipped we will need to take the dog to our holding kennels to obtain the details, and the owner will be contacted.

Where they are held

Strays are held in our care at our holding kennels in Leconfield:

Leconfield Kennels (external website)

Dogs will be kept at the kennels for seven days to allow the owners time to collect them.  After seven days, the council will look to rehome the dog, however you may still be able to reclaim your dog if it is waiting to be rehomed.

Costs

There is a charge for kennelling and for returning a dog to its owner. For the first three days of kennelling the charge is £50.00, and after that the daily charge is £9.00. The owner can collect their dog directly from the kennels, or alternatively the dog can be returned by the council to the owners property for a charge of £43.00.  If you are collecting your dog from Leconfield Kennels, please take identification, for example a passport, driver's licence or utility bill.

Banned dogs

It is a criminal offence to own or keep any of the following types of dog: 

  • Pit Bull Terrier
  • Japanese Tosa
  • Dogo Argentino
  • Fila Brasiliero 


Such dogs should be reported to the Police by calling Tel: 101

Does the council offer dog neutering and micro-chipping?

The council does not offer help with neutering your dog.

However, the Dogs Trust offers a discounted rate for dog neutering at local vets for people who qualify for certain means-tested benefits.  Further details are available on the Dogs Trust website. 

The Dogs Trust - low cost neutering (external website)

The council does arrange events where micro-chipping takes place, and these will be advertised locally when on offer.

The next free micro-chipping event is being organised by the Dogs Trust at BATA Country Store in Tickton on 24 February 2016, and includes a basic health-check for dogs.  The following pdf document contains further details about the event.

Free micro-chipping event in Tickton - 24 February 2016 (pdf 697kb opens in new window)

Why micro-chip?

From April 2016, all dogs in England will need to be micro-chipped.  This will ensure that your contact details can be easily traced should your pet ever be lost or stolen. A small chip is inserted under your dog's skin, which can be read by a special scanner to quickly find its owner's name and and address.  Dogs on a highway or in a public place must also wear a collar and tag with the name and address of the owner inscribed.
