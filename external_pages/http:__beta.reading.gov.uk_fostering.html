Fostering

If you think you could give a caring home to a child, children or a young person even if it is only for a weekend every now and then you should get in touch with us and find out more about fostering.

You can read about our  Fostering Service: statement of purpose [166kb] to find out more about how the service works in Reading. You might also find our  staying put policy [229kb] information useful.

Could I foster?

To become a foster carer you must:

  • Be over 21 years old
  • Have at least one spare bedroom.

We have foster carers of all kinds: with children of their own, or not; from any ethnic group or cultural background; married, living with their partner or single; working or unemployed. We would like to welcome you to join our community of foster carers if you feel you could offer a safe and caring home to a Reading child.

What different fostering schemes are there?

Fostering can be anything from taking a child into your home for a weekend every now and then to looking after them on a full-time basis for months or years.

You could find yourself looking after a young child full-time for a few months or up to two years, offering a short break for a disabled child or providing supported lodgings for a young person of 16 to 18 years old. Maybe you could also consider offering a permanent home to a child or young person until they are ready to move on to independence.

The recruitment and assessment team will help you to identify which of the following schemes will be most suitable to your skills and lifestyle:

  • Short-term fostering -  from one night to two years
  • Long-term fostering -  two years or more
  • Supported lodgings - for young people on the road to independence
  • Respite fostering - to relieve other foster carers
  • Emergency fostering - for a couple of nights or up to 28 days
  • Short breaks - for children with disabilities and their families, to give them a regular break from one another.
     

How do I start the fostering journey?

We run a number of information events throughout the year. At these you will meet members of the Recruitment and Assessment  Team and other foster carers, and find out more about becoming a carer.

You can let us know you are interested in becoming a foster carer by filling in our online fostering and carer scheme enquiry form or calling us on 0118 937 3740.

We will talk to you about your interest in fostering and send you a detailed information pack. If you are still keen to foster after reading the pack, you should contact the Recruitment and Assessment Team duty worker who will arrange a home visit. During the visit your whole household will have the opportunity to meet with an experienced member of our fostering team and for you both to decide if fostering is the right decision for you at this time.

Once your application has been accepted, the assessment process will begin. It involves a 20-hour Skills To Foster course that you are required to fully attend.

The assessment process is divided into two stages:

  • Stage One - checks and references will be carried out and you will need to have a medical with your GP. When these are complete a decision will be made on whether to progress to Stage two.
  • Stage Two - this includes eight to ten home visits and homework to be completed by you. All of this will form part of a report that will be presented to Reading's Fostering Panel.

The Fostering Panel will use the report we make during Stages One and Two to consider your suitability as a foster carer. You have to attend when the panel meets to discuss this.

Will you support me while I'm fostering?

You will have support from the beginning of the fostering journey. You will have an allocated social worker who will be able to give you support and guidance.

We will also buddy you up with an experienced foster carer at the beginning of the assessment process who will be give you support through the assessment and for your first year as a foster carer. An ongoing mentoring scheme is available as required.

You can always refer to our  foster care handbook [1Mb] which will give you advice on a number of commonly occurring queries. You may also find our  Foster Carer Charter [279kb] of use.

You will also have access to an extensive range of support services outside normal office hours and support groups. There is also the opportunity for your own children to engage with the Children Who Foster group.

As an approved foster carer you will become a member of Fostering Network who can offer independent advice. You will also have access to Research in Practice, a website containing a wealth of research and evidence, as well as resources to help you keep up to date with shifting policy and practice. Find out more here.

Will I get financial support?

You will receive a fee for caring for the child. The amount you receive is calculated according to your experience and availability.

When a child is placed with you we will also give you an allowance to meet the needs of the child, this will vary depending on the age of the child.

Will I get training?

Once you are approved you are required to take part in five core training courses:

  • Universal Safeguarding
  • Health and Safety
  • Paediatric First Aid
  • Understanding Loss and Attachment
  • Communicating with Children.

Further training is available for all of our foster carers. The foster carer training courses cover a wide variety of subjects: children's mental health matters, food safety, the importance of culture, amongst other things.

Can I foster a relative's child?

There are two situations that could lead to you fostering a relative's child:

  • Informal family care: an arrangement made between the parent and a relative, which doesn't involve the Council. This is a private arrangement made between a parent and carer. A close relative is defined as step-parent, grandparent, brother, sister, uncle, aunt (whether fully-related, half-related or related by marriage/civil partnership).
  • Family and friends foster care: if the Council place a child into the care of a relative. The relative becomes an approved foster carer and the child becomes 'looked after'.

What is private fostering?

Private fostering is not the same as fostering. It is when a child is cared for by a person who is not their parent or close relative for 28 days or more. In this case a close relative is defined as a grandparent, brother, sister, uncle or aunt. It also includes half relatives, step parents and people who are related to the child by marriage.

Legally, the parent and carer have to tell us if they have made this arrangement. It also means we can give help and support to the carer and child, if necessary.

For information please read our  private fostering pamphlet [629kb], our  Private Fostering Statement of Purpose [244kb] or contact the Family and Friends Service on 0118 937 3740.

What can I expect as a looked after child or young person?

If you can't live with your own family we will make sure you are looked after and cared for. We have made a pledge - this is like a set of promises - about what we, and the organisations we work with, will do to support you as a young person in care with Reading and what you can expect from us.  There are two versions of the pledge depending on your age:

  •  Reading Children in Care Pledge - ages 11 and over [421kb]
  •  Reading Children in Care Pledge - children under 11 years [75kb].

If you feel unhappy you can tell your carer, social worker or another grown up you trust, or you can use the MOMO app or website to tell us.

The Reading Youth website has lots of information to help you as a young person in care.  This includes information on activities and events going on across Reading which you might like to take part in as well as information about being in care. 

Finally, the fostering team has also made a downloadable leaflet called the  Children's Guide [558kb], about what you can expect from the service.
