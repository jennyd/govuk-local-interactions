Hazardous substances

Environmental Health Last updated - July 2014

Asbestos cement

Asbestos cement is a well bonded, sheet building material, commonly used for roofing and outbuildings such as garages. Care must be taken not to confuse it with asbestos insulation board which is similar in appearance, but much more hazardous since it is softer.

Advice for dealing with asbestos cement (only)

Breathing asbestos dust is dangerous. Any work on asbestos cement may generate asbestos dust and we advise that the following precautions are taken to minimise the risk.

  • It is advised that a sample is taken and analysed by a UKAS accredited laboratory to identify the type of asbestos present.
  • Avoid creating dust from asbestos cement.
  • Never sand down or wire-brush asbestos cement.
  • Do not use power tools on asbestos cement.
  • Avoid breaking asbestos cement.
  • When working with asbestos cement it is advisable to wear disposable overalls and a face mask. Check that they are suitable for use with asbestos. You can get advice from safety equipment centres. After use, these should be disposed of as asbestos waste.
  • When removing asbestos cement, thoroughly wet it first, particularly around any fixings. Use an ordinary hosepipe or watering can for this, not a high-powered water jet. Add detergent to the water if possible.
  • If fixings are difficult to undo, try to cut them off rather than break the asbestos.

Where to dispose of your asbestos cement

KNH property

If you discover asbestos do not attempt work involving sprayed asbestos, lagging or insulating boards, as this must be undertaken by a licensed asbestos removal contractor.

  • KNH advice on asbestos

Private property

Advice on removal & disposal of asbestos cement products

Asbestos cement waste must be wrapped in a double layer of strong polythene and loose edges sealed with tape before being disposed of.

Private householders should take their asbestos waste to the Household Waste Recycling Centre at Emerald Street, Huddersfield. You must ring the site at least 12 hours before you visit, on: 01484 536967
If you can not get through please ring: 01484 226886

Trade or business

Commercial operators need to be registered carriers and will need to fill in a consignment note before disposing of asbestos cement, you can get these from the Environment Agency. Commercial operators must contact the Environment Agency 24 hours before you want to visit and they will give you instructions on how to deal with asbestos prior to disposal. 0113 244 0191

Radon

Radon is a radioactive gas that occurs naturally. It has no taste, smell or colour, in fact special devices are needed to detect it. Radon is everywhere, but usually in insignificant quantities.

Where radon comes from

When Uranuim decays it becomes radium, when radium decays it becomes radon. Uranium is found in small quantities in all rocks and soil, but amounts vary from place to place, and even between neighbouring buildings.

Radon rises from soil into the air. Outdoors the radon is diluted and the risk it poses is negligible, however, when it stays in enclosed spaces concentrations can build up.

Tests carried out a few years ago revealed that certain properties in a particular area of Kirklees (the former HD7 postcode area) had radon levels slightly above average background levels.

Testing for radon

The only way to check radon levels within a particular house is to expose a test kit from PHE (Public Health England) and have the results analysed.

The test kits currently cost £49.80 and are available from Public Health England who also issue further guidance booklets, which can be found on the PHE website.

Affects on buying and selling property

If you are buying or selling a house your solicitor may suggest a radon bond, an amount of money they hold pending the test results. Depending on the test results this money is either released to the seller or utilised to carry out remedial works by the purchaser.
