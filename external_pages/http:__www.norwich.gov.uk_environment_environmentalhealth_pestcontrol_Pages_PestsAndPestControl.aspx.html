Pests and pest control

Pests that can be dealt with by Norwich City Council

We provide a pest control service to households for the eradication of the following pests. There are charges for this service, except for the treatment of cockroaches.

  • Bedbugs
  • Fleas
  • Mice
  • Rats
  • Wasps
  • Cockroaches (no charge)

To arrange an appointment with a pest control officer, complete the online contact us form or call 0344 980 3333.

Other pests

  • Insects - ants, bees and booklice
  • Foxes, bats and squirrels
  • Pigeons

Commercial and landlord pest control services

Pest control services required for these properties can be dealt with by Norwich Norse Environmental.

For further information or to arrange a quote, please contact http://norwichnorseenv.co.uk/ or call 01603 894140

Bedbugs

  • £35 inspection fee. This fee will be deducted from the treatment price if bedbugs are identified and a treatment is booked. If bedbugs are not identified this fee is non-refundable.
  • £130 for one treatment and monitoring visits for a three bedroom property
  • £70 for residents receiving benefits, one treatment and monitoring visit for a three bedroom property
  • £30 additional rooms

Additional treatments at the same property will be charged again at the above rates.

Bedbug infestations are becoming more common, the reasons for this are not certain, though increased travel abroad may be a factor.

Bedbugs are visible to the human eye. Adult bedbugs are approximately 5mm across reddish brown in colour and are similar in size to a ladybird. They thrive in warm surroundings, are small bloodsucking insects that live in cracks and crevices. They are usually found around the bed where they have a ready source of food as the user settles to sleep. Bedbugs can, however, migrate to other parts of the room. Other indications of bedbug activity may include live or dead bedbugs and blood spotting. They may be initially observed on bed linen but can be noticed along mattress seams and other places such as headboards.

Some people suffer a reaction to the bite although it is important to note that there is no evidence to suggest that they spread diseases.

If you think that you have bedbugs it is important that you act promptly, they won’t go away and if left untreated can be easily spread between family and friends.
 
The pest control officer will survey the property to evaluate the problem and will provide advice as to how to proceed with the treatment.
 
To arrange an appointment with a pest controller contact the customer contact team on 0344 980 3333.

Fleas

  • £90 treatment for only, one visit,  for a three bedroom property
  • £50 for residents receiving benefits, treatment only, one visit, for a three bedroom property
  • £147.50 for emergency same day treatment (subject to availability)
  • £20 additional rooms

The most effective way to get rid of animal fleas is to make sure pets and their bedding are regularly treated. Vets can advise about this. If there is a serious problem, please contact us to arrange for the whole house to be treated with insecticide.

The pest control officer will need access to floor areas which must be cleared and vacuumed thoroughly. The insecticide does not harm furnishings and will be sprayed over all the floor areas in affected rooms. Although the insecticide used is as harmless to humans as it is possible to use, it is particularly important to remove children’s toys from the areas to be sprayed. Rooms must be vacated before the treatment begins and left as long as possible afterwards before being vacuumed.

Mice

  • £28.50 for an initial assessment and up to three further visits. Any subsequent visits as part of the same treatment will be charged at £13.50 for each additional visit.
  • £55 for emergency same day treatment (subject to availability)

Mice will come in from outside to find shelter and food. They can get in through very small holes as small as 6mm and will thrive if they can hide and can get food. It is important therefore to make entry difficult by filling in any small holes (e.g. around waste water pipes) and by keeping work surfaces and floors clear of food debris, especially at night. With people out of the way, the mice will feel free to come out and find food.

Although infestations happen in the cleanest of homes, as with many infestation problems, keeping a house clean and tidy and in good repair will make an infestation less likely and easier to treat if a problem occurs.

Most infestations can be easily dealt with using products purchased at a hardware store or supermarket. If you find that you cannot deal with the problem please call the customer contact team for an appointment with a pest control officer.

Mice in gardens, garages and outbuildings (if not connected to the house), cannot be treated by the council's pest controllers.

Rats

  • £28.50 for an initial assessment and up to three further visits. Any subsequent visits as part of the same treatment will be charged at £13.50 for each additional visit.
  • £55 for emergency same day treatment (subject to availability)

Rats commonly live in sewers, or are found in holes in banks of earth.

Rats, or signs of them, (droppings, gnawed wood or flex, disturbed food, scrabbling in the loft or walls), inside homes, will almost certainly have come from the drainage system. Common points to watch for are broken drain covers, unprotected rainwater down pipes at roof level (a wire cage fitted inside the top of the down pipe will stop rats getting out), dry outside toilets and uncapped, disused drains. It is important to keep drainage systems in good repair.

Rats like easy food. For them fast food is a rotting compost heap, food put out for the birds, animal food (where there are chickens or rabbits for example) and food litter dropped on to the street.

If there is a problem in an area with rats emerging from the sewer, or living aboveground, easy food will attract them into gardens. Put bird food out of reach where the rats cannot climb up to get to it. Stop feeding the birds while rats are being treated. Do not over-feed animals so food is not left for rats to find. Regularly clean up after animals. Use an enclosed compost bin. Do not put cooked food into the compost. Put litter into a bin. Do not leave household refuse outside, except in a closed bin, for longer than is absolutely necessary.

If rats have been attracted to an area they find it easier to stay if there is somewhere to hide such as overgrown gardens, under garden sheds and decking. If they are found to be there, clearing the overgrown areas, and blocking access to hiding places will help to get rid of them. However, unless they are attracted there in the first place, such conditions will not of themselves cause a problem.

What does the council do? 

The council works with Anglian Water to deal with rats in the sewers. The council regularly clears the streets of litter and dropped food waste. Where property owners are not keeping their drains and sewers repaired, the council uses legal remedies to require the work to be done. A pest control officer and housing officer will work together to sort out a problem with rats affecting homes rented from the council.

Please call the customer contact team for an appointment with a pest control officer.

Wasps

  • £67 for treatment of a wasp nest
  • £40 for residents receiving benefits
  • £22.50 for additional nests at the same address
  • £125.50 for emergency same day treatment (subject to availability).

Wasps are natural predators that eat a variety of garden pests. As a general rule, unless they are in a difficult position such as over window or doorway, they are best left. Requests to destroy wasps' nests tend to be received in late summer, when their food - things like rotting fruit on trees and branches - is running out.

The council can destroy wasps' nests but it's often best to let nature take its course. A good cold snap will destroy a wasps' nest.

Please call the customer contact team for an appointment with a pest control officer.

Ants, bees and booklice

Ants

Ants are a common problem and although they can be a nuisance they are not harmful to health.


The best way to get rid of them is to destroy the nest with boiling water. The nest should be exposed and destroyed by pouring in liberal quantities of boiling water, or use a proprietary insecticide. Read the instructions on the label carefully

If the nest cannot be located treat in the area where the ants are most active with a proprietary ant killer. This may be a dust, spray or gel application. It can take a few weeks to get rid of the problem. Use according to the instructions on the packaging and be very careful not to contaminate food. Never use insecticide sprays in enclosed spaces

Bees

Norwich City Council does not treat bees because they play an important role in helping to maintain the environment. The following advice is based on information from the Norfolk Beekeepers' Association website.

Solitary bees

Solitary bees may be seen flying from holes in brickwork or on the side of buildings or from holes in the ground, where there may be a large number of holes grouped together. Bees start to fly in April or May and cease flying in autumn time. The size of solitary bees vary from 5mm to 20mm depending on the species. Solitary bees do not sting or swarm and they pollinate a wide range of flora and therefore should be encouraged and left alone
.

Bumble bees

Bumblebees are often seen flying from holes in the ground, from tussocks of grass, from under sheds and occasionally from bird boxes. They tend to be roundish in shape and often black with white/yellow/gold stripe on their bodies. They vary in size from 15mm to 30mm depending on the species. Nests start small in April and gradually increase in size to a maximum of 200 to 300 bees by late summer. Bumble bees do not swarm and will only sting if provoked so a nest will not normally cause any problems.   

Honey bees

Honey bees do swarm and a swarm may consist of a large number of bees, many hundred or even many thousands clustered on a branch, tree, bush etc. Honey bees can suddenly arrive as a large cloud of bees and can be quite noisy. When settled, bees will may be seen flying to and from the cluster as they search for a new permanent home. If they are left alone they will normally move on within a couple of days or so. Honey bees do sting and will do so if provoked. If they are left alone there should be no problems. The occupier of the property should seek professional advice from a beekeeper concerning their removal and should not attempt to tackle the swarm themselves.  For advice and information on honey bee swarms in the Norwich area, please visit the Norfolk Beekeepers' Association website

 

Booklice

 or psocids can sometimes be seen in food packaging or flour that has been kept in a cupboard for too long. They are not harmful. All affected food needs to be thrown away (in the household refuse) and cupboards cleaned and thoroughly dried. To prevent further problems ensure that food is stored in dry conditions and not kept past the use by date.

Foxes, bats and squirrels

Foxes

Although not always welcome foxes are not regarded as pests and the law provides a considerable amount of protection for them. More detailed information and advice on how to deter them can be found using the links:

The Fox Project
Norfolk Wildlife Trust

Bats

Bats and their roosts are protected under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. It is illegal for anyone without a licence to intentionally disturb, injure or kill a wild bat, to possess or exchange a bat, or to intentionally damage or obstruct access to any place that a bat uses for shelter or protection.
 
If essential work needs to be carried where bats and their roosts (even if empty) are likely to be disturbed contact the government’s Statutory Nature Conservation Organisation (SNCO) before the work is started. Advice will be given as to how the work can be done without breaking the law. Details of how to contact an SNCO are available here. For more information about bats and how to contact an SNCO visit the Bat Conservation website. 

Squirrels

The council does not provide a service to deal with squirrels. Residents are advised to contact a pest control company (for example by looking in Yellow Pages) to remove them if they get into the house.

Pigeons

Feral pigeons are common throughout Britain and cause many problems especially in towns cities like Norwich.

The main problems are:

  • nesting on and within buildings
  • fouling of buildings and monuments. The acid in the droppings reacts with chemicals in stonework and causes erosion of the surface
  • fouling of pavements underneath where they roost and perch causing slippery surfaces
  • mites and insects in pigeon droppings that have been allowed to build up
  • risk of disease is slight but pigeons are known to carry Salmonella (a cause of food poisoning) and Ornithosis (a mild form of Psittacosis – a lung infection). They can also cause allergies in some people through contact with their droppings, parasites, dandruff and feathers.

Pigeon control

Norwich City Council does not currently have a culling programme for pigeons.


The most important factor in controlling the number of pigeons in any area is to control their food supply as pigeons can breed for most of the year because they have a year round food supply. Pigeons will eat not only food put out for them and other birds but also waste fast food, and food from split rubbish bags.

The food they eat affects their health, as it may lack essential nutrients and the risk of injury is increased, as they are attracted to the built up areas in the city. They may for example catch their feet in the man-made structures.

Attracting pigeons to the garden can deprive smaller birds of food and scare them away. Pigeons carry viruses that can pass to other birds. Food put out for pigeons can attract rats and mice if not eaten.

Pigeon numbers will go down naturally to match the food available to them. In the same way that numbers increase as more food is supplied.
