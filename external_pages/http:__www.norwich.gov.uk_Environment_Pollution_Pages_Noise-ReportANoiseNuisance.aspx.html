Complaints about noise levels

The number of complaints about noise increases year on year. In 2013 the council received 3641 noise complainants and, of those, 3490 were resolved. These complaints mostly concerned unacceptable night time noise from neighbours - typically loud music, televisions, video games and late night/early morning DIY. We also deal with complaints about noise from car alarms, burglar alarms, construction sites and plant machinery (such as air conditioning units).

Most noise complaints are investigated but there are some that cannot be dealt with. These include some human based noise such as shouting, swearing, loud talking; general domestic noises such as banging of doors, walking on laminate floors etc and noise from aircraft (except model aircraft)

Anticipating problems

We try to anticipate where problems may occur to reduce the likelihood of complaints. We review a large number of planning applications each year in order to minimise the number of noise complaints from commercial premises and also look at the potential impact of noise from new developments.

We also review and comment on premises licences for pubs and clubs. We may set music levels or request other conditions to minimise noise breakout. If necessary we investigate and take action against any establishment causing a noise nuisance.

How to make a complaint?

If anyone has a problem with noise from a neighbour, it is usually better to talk to them first. They may not realise they are causing a problem and the matter could be resolved quickly and easily.

If, however, you do not want to speak to your neighbour or you have but there has been no improvement, then call the council on 0344 980 3333 or email info@norwich.gov.uk 

What happens with a domestic noise complaint?

When we receive a complaint, a visit will be made to the person alleged to be responsible for the noise or we write to them. The person will be advised that a complaint has been made but not of who made the complaint. Most of the time this solves the problem. Often people are unaware that they are causing a noise nuisance or they stop once they are advised that action can be taken against them for causing a nuisance to neighbours.

We also ask the complainant to keep a record of how often and for how long the problem occurs. This information is required should the case be taken further but it also gives us an idea of the type and severity of the problem and the best course of action to deal with it.
If the advisory visit or letter is unsuccessful and the noise continues, we will investigate further. A neighbourhood warden or other council officer will need to visit when the nuisance is occurring to witness the noise. Once witnessed, if the noise is found to be a statutory nuisance an abatement notice will be served on the person responsible. The notice requires the person to stop making the noise, usually with immediate effect, and informs them that failing to comply with this requirement can result in prosecution and a fine of up to £5,000.
In some cases, the council will apply to the Magistrate’s Court for a warrant to enter premises, if need be without the owner/occupiers consent, in order to seize the equipment causing the noise. Under these circumstances, the equipment is usually retained pending the outcome of prosecution.
The council can also carry out work to stop a noise from happening, such as physically silencing an alarm. All costs incurred are recharged back to the person responsible for the misfiring alarm.
Where enforcement action is taken in response to a noise nuisance, this is only done when the noise is considered to be a statutory noise nuisance by an authorised officer. We do not rely solely on the word of the complainant. In order to qualify as a statutory noise nuisance the noise would need to be sufficiently intrusive to disrupt the enjoyment of someone’s property. This disturbance can include garden areas. A statutory noise nuisance can be considered at any time of day or night. A neighbour is therefore not expected to be completely quiet and inaudible and hence a level of tolerance is also required.
If the person responsible for the noise nuisance is a council tenant, our Housing dept will also be informed of the problem as any substantiated noise nuisance will also be a breach of tenancy. Housing will take their own action in tandem with any enforcement action taken by the Environmental Protection team. More severe cases of anti-social behavior, including noise nuisance, can be elevated to our ABATE team.

Being a good neighbour – consideration and awareness 

Most noise problems are resolved when the person responsible for the noise is advised that they may be causing a nuisance. It seems that many people just do not realise they are disturbing their neighbours. Nowadays, more people live closer together, use gardens as outdoor living areas, and are less likely to know their neighbours. Often people do not realise the effect that noise has on others.  
Please be a considerate neighbour. Keep TV, radio, music system and video game volumes to reasonable levels, or use headphones (but not so loud as to damage your hearing!). A good rule of thumb is if you can hear your music etc outside your home, then it is most likely loud enough to disturb a neighbour. 
Keep loud DIY to times when neighbours are not likely to be trying to sleep, and preferably give your neighbor prior warning if you need to do some noisy work so they know when to expect it. 
 
Ensure dogs are happy when left alone - it sometimes helps to leave the radio on for example, but not too loud. If the dog is to be regularly left alone for long periods, consider if this is fair on the dog or neighbours before becoming a dog owner. Dogs can be trained at any age not to bark indiscriminately. Bear in mind you may not always know how your dog behaves when you are out, nor how stressed it can become in your absence. A good idea may be to get a friend to look in and check the dog if you are going to be out for some time.
 
House alarms should be regularly serviced and repaired if necessary. If away from home ask a friend to have responsibility for the house and the alarm.  
 
If your car alarm is apt to go off unexpectedly, ask a garage to find out why and to repair the fault. 
 
This may all sound like common sense but from the number of calls the council receives it appears common sense is frequently not applied. Please think about how you would feel if your neighbour did not consider your feelings or respect your home and environment.

Construction/demolition noise?

This type of noise may be controlled by planning condition but usually only on large developments. Whatever the size of project, if you are disturbed by unreasonable amounts of noise or if the noise is occurring outside of normal working hours ie before 7.30am or after 5.30pm Monday-Saturday, then call the council on 0344 980 3333 or email info@norwich.gov.uk. Ordinarily we would not expect construction or demolition work to be carried out on a Sunday or a Bank Holiday.
If a developer or contractor is found to be operating outside of their planning condition or causing unreasonable amounts of noise outside of expected working hours, the council will take appropriate action to stop the nuisance from re-occurring.

Noise from aircraft

We have no powers to deal with noise from aircraft. To complain about aircraft noise please contact the relevant airport management or operator:

  • Civil aircraft noise - the Department for Transport is responsible for the policy on civil aircraft noise in the UK. Contact: 
     

    Department for Transport
    76 Marsham Street
    London
    SW1P 4DR
    t: 0300 330 3000

  • military aircraft - complaints about noise from military aircraft should be made to any RAF station.
  • Police helicopter - complaints about noise from the Police helicopter should be made to Police Headquarters on 0845 456 4567.  

Noise from the railways

We only have very limited powers to deal with noise from the railway. If you have a problem with noise from trains please contact the local train operator on 0800 022 3720 or customer.relations@nationalrail.co.uk or Norwich train station.

Loud music from vehicles

If you are being disturbed by loud music from a vehicle that is parked on someone’s property, ie they are playing their music while working on their vehicle, it may be possible to investigate the problem as a domestic noise nuisance. In these circumstances contact the council on 0344 980 3333 or email info@norwich.gov.uk. Do however try to speak to your neighbour first of all and especially if it happens infrequently as they may not realise how far sound travels outside.
If the vehicle is in a public area or on the public highway, it is a matter for the Police as they have powers under Regulation 97 of the Road Vehicles (Construction & Use) Regulations 1986 to prohibit the use of a vehicle emitting avoidable excessive noise.
