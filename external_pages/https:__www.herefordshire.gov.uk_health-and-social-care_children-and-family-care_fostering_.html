Fostering

Local foster families for local children

Fostering is a way of providing a family life for children who cannot live with their own families. Their families might be going through a difficult time or be ill.

There is a huge need for more foster carers so that children can be matched with families who best reflect the child's background and culture and so that, wherever possible, children can be placed near to their own families, schools and communities. 

We need all kinds of carers to offer a range of placements from long and short term to specialist pieces of work with children facing challenges in their lives. We will offer you an excellent financial package and all the local training and support you need at any time of day.

Could I become a foster carer?

You could be a foster carer if:

  • You are over 21 (there is no upper age limit)
  • You have some experience of children and/or young people
  • You are single, married or living with a partner of either gender
  • You have a healthy lifestyle
  • You can foster even if you do or don't have children of your own, your life experiences and skills are important
  • You can continue to work and foster at the same time

As long as you have time to meet a child's needs, we can work with you to become a foster carer.

Why should I become a foster carer with the council?

We will offer you an excellent financial package and all the local training and support you need at any time of day. Fostering with Herefordshire Council is a great way to support local children in the county.

We offer:

  • A reliable and competitive fee
  • Equipment for the child plus a generous allowance
  • An excellent programme of local workshops and training
  • Your own social worker
  • Respite breaks, monthly local support groups
  • 24 hour support
  • Membership to the Fostering Network
  • 'Buddy' scheme linking you to local experienced carers
  • And so much more

What does a foster carer do?

Fostering isn't easy but is extremely satisfying. 

Helping vulnerable young people feel secure and good about themselves makes the experience worthwhile and is highly rewarding. 

Foster carers:

  • Promote the healthy growth and development of children
  • Provide a safe and caring environment, keeping children from harm
  • Encourage children to take part in school, further education, training or employment
  • Promote positive behaviour and act as an advocate

Be part of the team.

  • You won't be alone and would be working with a team of people, including social workers and other professionals
  • Taking part in meetings and help make decisions about the child's care
  • There are many local training courses to help you develop your skills and learning
  • You would be working with parents to encourage and supervise contact. Most children keep regular contact with their families
  • For many children the plan is for them to return home and their parents still have some responsibilities for them
  • Fostering can be for a few days, weeks, months or long term for years until a child becomes an adult

Check out the foster care stories page for real experiences.

What is the process of becoming a foster carer?

  • Get in touch with us and begin to find out if fostering is right for you. It's easy - just give us a call on 01432 383240
  • A social worker will come to your home and talk about fostering in more detail. Helping you to make a decision
  • Complete an application and we make more home visits, and meet the other members of your household and people who know you. We carry out assessments and checks and you’ll start to get trained in becoming a foster carer
  • You and your social worker attend a fostering panel who will make a recommendation about your suitability to foster. The fostering service will then make a decision based on this recommendation
  • If you are successful your social worker makes all the arrangements for a child to come and stay with you

It doesn't end there - we are always here to help.

Why do some children need fostering?

Fostering is a way of providing a family life for children who cannot live with their own parents. Their parents might be going through a difficult time or be ill.

Often children will return home once the problems that caused them to come into foster care have been resolved. 

Fostering helps children prepare for the next move. That could be returning home, long term fostering, becoming an independent adult or finding a new family through adoption.

A child can need foster care for many reasons:

  • A member of the family has a drug or alcohol related problem which seriously affects the safety or care of the child
  • There are major problems and tensions in the family and children feel that they need to be away from home for a while
  • The child has been physically or sexually abused or seriously neglected by someone in the home
  • Families of disabled children or children with behavioural difficulties may benefit from short breaks, where the child enjoys time in a new environment and the parents have some time to themselves
  • A young person has been remanded into foster care by the court
  • A child is an unaccompanied refugee or asylum seeker

There is a huge need for more foster carers so that children can be matched with families who best reflect the child's background and culture and so that, wherever possible, children can be placed near to their own families, schools and communities.  

What are the types of foster care?

Short term or long term care

A child might come into care because of an illness or accident or a serious incident at home. Whatever the reasons, social services will be working with the family to resolve the issues so that the child can return home as soon as possible.

Some children are unable to return to their families. They will require a stable, long term fostering placement until they reach the age of 18 or beyond.

Family and friends

Family and friends carers play a key role in allowing children to remain living with adults that they know and trust if, for whatever reason, they cannot live with their birth parents. In such cases Herefordshire Council is committed to ensuring that, where possible, a child or young person can remain living within their own family network. 

The support that family and friends carers receive is critical to the success of any placement.

Brothers and sisters

Wherever possible siblings should be placed together. For many foster children, the relationship with their brothers and sisters is what they value most when separated from their family and staying together is very important.

Disabled children

We need carers with experience of caring for children with physical or learning difficulties and other additional needs.

Parent and child

We need carers who can help to give extra support in their home to new parents and their babies, particularly teenage mums.

Emergency placement

We need foster carers who have an interest in and an understanding of children coming from other cultures and can provide short periods of care until plans are made for their future.

We need places for children that need somewhere safe to stay for a few nights.

Older children

We need carers who can foster teenagers. Carers who can inspire and provide them with an environment to help them build independent living skills and help them build a positive future.

  • Can you be a role model to a young person?
  • Can you inspire and encourage them to grow into young adults?

Herefordshire Intensive Placement Support Service (HIPSS)

Herefordshire Intensive Placement Support Service (HIPSS) is a therapeutic fostering service to children and young people looked after by Herefordshire Council with the most complex needs that would normally need to be cared for in residential care, away from their local communities and networks.

Therapeutic foster care gives children and young people who have experienced significant trauma in their lives an opportunity to overcome adversity and have the chance to form stable and secure relationships with their carers and live appropriately ordinary lives.

HIPSS is a dedicated friendly, creative team of professionals from psychology, education, social care, fostering and youth work. We work with children and young people aged 7-18 years with complex needs.

Learn more about HIPSS

Private fostering

Private fostering is when a child or young person under 16 (or 18 if disabled) goes to live for 28 days or more with someone who is not a:

  • Parent (including step parent)
  • Legal guardian or person with parental responsibility
  • Close relative (like grandparent, brother or sister, aunt or uncle)

Learn more about private fostering

​Supported lodgings 

Young people often need support with becoming independent. Especially those people who have lived in care or have been homeless. Supported lodgings provide a safe place for them to become confident and learn about living as an adult.

Learn more about supported lodgings

Short breaks scheme

The short breaks scheme provides overnight, family-based, short break provision for children with disabilities, allowing them to have a new experience and for their families to have some rest.

​Anyone can be a short breaks carer. There are no specialist skills or qualifications required. However, we would be particularly interested in hearing from people with professional or personal experience of children with disabilities. We will train you and provide all the support you need. All we ask is that you are understanding, patient and committed to caring for a child who has disabilities.

Being a short break carer is a highly rewarding challenge. If you think you have what it takes, get in touch with us to find out more.

Learn more about becoming a short break carer

I am already a foster carer with someone else. Can I transfer?

You can transfer from another local authority or from an independent fostering agency to Herefordshire Council. If you currently have foster children you can bring them with you.

The process is easy and there will be no disruption in your support.

I am in foster care and would like to speak to someone?

If there's anything that you are not happy about, it is very important that you tell an adult that you trust, such as a relative, teacher or social worker.

If you want to have some help in saying what you think or you want to complain, then you can contact the National Youth Advocacy Service (NYAS) on Freephone 0808 808 1001 or you can email them at help@nyas.net 

How do I complain about the fostering service?

The fostering service work to achieve the highest standards. However, if you feel unhappy in any way, we ask that you speak to us or your social worker about it. It is only by you telling us what is wrong that we can work to put it right and improve.
