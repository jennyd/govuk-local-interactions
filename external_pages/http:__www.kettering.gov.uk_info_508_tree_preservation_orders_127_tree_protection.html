Tree Protection

Tree Preservation Orders (TPO)

Use our interactive mapping to view locations of conservation areas and tree preservation orders.

Local Councils are able to make Tree Preservation Orders (TPOs) in order to protect trees and woodlands, usually on the grounds that they make an important contribution to the local landscape, but perhaps because of some other special quality, such as rarity or historic significance. TPOs are usually made when it is felt in the public interest to ensure that the trees or woodlands are not felled or pruned inappropriately. The TPO does not prevent work from being done, but requires that the consent of the Local Council be obtained.

If you wish to carry out work to a tree protected by a TPO you must first apply to the Borough Council for approval. A standard guidance pack and application form are available for this purpose. If you are unable to print the document we will be able to provide it on request. You may apply yourself, or ask your tree work contractor to do so on your behalf. A visit will be made to inspect the tree, and the Council will make a decision within eight weeks of receiving your application.

It is an offence to carry out work to a tree protected by a TPO, or remove it, without the approval of the Borough Council. If convicted in a Magistrate's Court, a fine of up to £20000 may result if the appearance or condition of the tree is affected beyond recovery (less damaging work may still result in a fine of up to £2500). If the case is referred to the Crown Court an unlimited fine may be imposed.

Courts have held that it is no defence to be unaware of the existence of a TPO, and it is possible to prosecute those who cause or permit unauthorised work, not just those who actually carry it out.

If a tree protected by a TPO has to be removed it is usual to require a replacement of an appropriate species in the same general location.

Development Services (01536 534316) will be pleased to offer advice on tree preservation orders and work to trees and it is often useful to arrange a site meeting before submitting an application.

If you wish to request to see a TPO in electronic format please email planning@kettering.gov.uk

Exemptions

There are certain exemptions from the requirement to obtain consent. Work to a tree, or part of a tree, which is dead or dangerous does not require the Council's permission. However, there may be other considerations - such as whether bats use the tree as a roosting site - and if so the work may be restricted by other regulations.

Anybody proposing to carry out work that they consider does not require the Council's permission should first notify the Council and, except in an emergency, allow five working days for the Council to satisfy itself that the work is exempt.

If the Council agrees that the work does not require an application for consent this does not mean that it is stating that no other regulations apply. For example, English Nature must be consulted if the tree is known to support a bat roost.
