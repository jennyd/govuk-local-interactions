Public Health & Statutory Nuisance

  1. Noise Nuisance
  2. Accumulations of domestic rubbish
  3. Bonfires
  4. Domestic Smoke / Smoke Control Areas
  5. Light pollution
  6. Dust
  7. Odour
  8. Condition of a property
  9. Blocked Drains

Environmental Health are able to deal with problems such as those above if they are a Statutory Nuisance or else Prejudicial to Health.

Statutory Nuisances - Whilst there is no definition in the legislation of what constitutes a statutory nuisance, it is based on the common law concept of what is to be regarded as an unreasonable interference with someone’s use of their own property. Good examples would be noise from barking dogs or loud music, smoky bonfires and unpleasant smells from accumulated rubbish.

Prejudicial to health - these types of complaint may be given priority by officers if there is an imminent risk to public health.  Good examples are noxious gases and fumes from burning waste, hazardous chemicals or asbestos waste. 

The vast majority of complaints received are about statutory nuisance - when determining if something is a nuisance a professional officer will typically consider the following:

  • Duration - i.e. how long it typically occurs
  • Frequency - how often it typically occurs
  • Impact – i.e. how it affects people, their health and well-being or material interference with reasonable use of their property
  • Local environment
  • Reasonable behaviour and Sensitivity of the complainant – the officer will have to consider if the “average” person would be affected and how reasonable a given activity is

Conducting an investigation

Nuisance investigations are serious matters and can be protracted - officers will often need to gather evidence over a period of time and may work with other departments or agencies to complete their investigation or achieve a desired outcome.  Often officers will require cooperation in undertaking their investigations - they may require you to keep logs or notes, or to allow access to your property at specified times in order to properly investigate.  If this cooperation is not given, it may not be possible to investigate or progress a complaint.  At some point the officer will make a professional judgement as to whether sufficient evidence exists to take action.   Officers cannot take action in every case and are not able to investigate complaints indefinitely - the law requires that a “reasonable” investigation is made. However officers will make every effort to ensure that investigations are thorough and if a nuisance is found to exist action will be taken.

 Anonymous complaints and Data Protection Act 1998

Environmental Health take all complaints seriously and want to resolve nuisance and public health issues - however for practical and legal reasons it may be very difficult and sometimes impossible to investigate anonymous complaints. You are advised at the very least to ask to speak to an officer to discuss a complaint.  The Council takes matters of Data Protection very seriously and there are strict penalties for breaching Data Protection requirements. With regard to nuisance complaints Environmental Health will not identify complainants- any such requests for personal information are strictly managed and are referred to the Councils System and Information Manager - typically there would need to be very good legal reasons to access this information.
