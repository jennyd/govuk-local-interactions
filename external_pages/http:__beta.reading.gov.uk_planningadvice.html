Planning

Most new buildings - or changes to buildings or the environment - require planning permission. We are responsible for deciding whether your development goes ahead.

Questions

Close allOpen all

Do I need planning permission?

If you want to work on or change a property, you might need to apply for planning permission. You do not always need  permission for minor works - this is called "permitted development rights". Check the Planning Portal website if you are not sure whether you need planning permission.

We can restrict permitted rights if we feel we need to. There are two ways we can do this:

  • By using a legal direction to remove rights from an area (known as an Article 4 direction)
  • By removing normal rights on a specific site as a condition of granting planning permission

There are various national permitted development rights to change uses without needing planning permission, as referred to on the Planning Portal site. If you want to make use of these rights, please note there are circumstances affecting parts of west and south Reading that mean that these permitted development rights do not apply.

Are there any local restrictions on whether I need planning permission (Article 4 Directions)?

An Article 4 direction removes some of the normal development rights that are available to property owners. We can issue these for various reasons to preserve elements of an area's appearance or character.

There are 17 Article 4 directions in Reading - 15 of these relate to patterned brickwork, with the other two relating to conversions from a house to a small house in multiple occupation.

In January 2016, the Council made a new Article 4 Direction for Jesse Terrace to control conversions from a house to a small house in multiple occupation (see below).

Small Houses in Multiple Occupation

A small house in multiple occupation - in the C4 planning use class - usually contains between three and six unrelated occupants who share basic amenities. You do not usually need planning permission to change a house into a small HMO (C3 use).

 An Article 4 direction [1Mb] is in place covering parts of Park, Redlands and Katesgrove Wards, which removes normal rights to convert a house (C3 use) into a small house in multiple occupation (C4 use). This means you will need planing permission to convert a house (C3) to a small house in multiple occupation (C4) within the area shown on the  Article 4 map [1Mb]. Conversion to a large house in multiple occupation - usually with more than 6 unrelated occupants - requires planning permission in any case across the whole Borough.

An Article 4 Direction was made in January 2016 to cover all properties in Jesse Terrace in Abbey Ward.   This Direction also removes normal rights to convert a house (C3 use) into a small house in multiple occupation (C4 use). This means you will need planning permission to convert a house (C3) fronting onto Jesse Terrace to a small house in multiple occupation (C4) within the area shown on the Article 4 Map. The Direction and a map of the area covered by the Direction can be viewed here:  Article 4 Direction - Jesse Terrace [313kb] and  Jesse Terrace Article 4 Map [532kb].

Download our  Q&A on small houses in multiple occupation [368kb] for more information.

Licensing of HMOs and Building Regulations are covered by separate legislation, and will need to be addressed separately.

Our  Supplementary Planning Document on Residential Conversions [2Mb] includes more information.

How do safety hazard areas affect whether I need planning permission?

Certain sites are excluded from permitted development rights under the Town and Country Planning (General Permitted Development) Order 1995. These are sites which fall within safety hazard areas, such as the Burghfield Atomic Weapons Establishment (AWE) middle (green) or outer (blue) consultation zones, as notified to the local planning authority by the Office for Nuclear Regulation (ONR), or of the Control of Major Accident Hazards (COMAH) zones which lie within Reading. See our  map of consultation zones [1Mb] for information on local sites.

The rights excluded are the following under Part 3 of the Order:

  • Class CA - A1 to A2 (deposit taker)
  • Class IA - A1 or A2 to C3
  • Class J - office to residential
  • Class K - various use classes to use as state funded school/nursery
  • Class L - agricultural building to flexible use
  • Class MA - agricultural building to school/nursery
  • Class MB - agricultural building to C3
  • Certain rights under Part 4 of the Order (temporary uses and buildings).

Many permitted development rights are subject to prior approval - but not if the site is in a safety hazard area. Developers should note that no advice can be given informally whether permitted development rights apply to a particular building. You must apply for a certificate of lawful use or development to confirm the development will be permitted and should obtain a positive prior approval decision or a certificate of lawfulness before committing to purchase or lease a building.

See our  Validation Checklist [539kb] to find out what information you will need to submit with your application.

Can I get any advice before applying for planning permission?

Yes - you can fill out a  pre-application enquiry form [240kb] for advice. How much we charge depends on your type of development and how much detail you need us to provide. This process should stop unacceptable schemes from entering the planning process.

How do I make a planning application?

Visit the Planning Portal website - you may make an online planning application, obtain application forms and it includes useful tools, such as a fee calculator and guidance on whether you need planning permission.

Fees may be paid when submitting a Planning Portal applications online or you can pay the planning fee online via our website.

Who decides whether I get planning permission?

Decisions on smaller applications are normally made by Planning Officers. Normally, only larger applications and applications referred by individual Councillors will be reported to the Council's Planning Applications Committee. Visit our Planning Applications Committee for more information and visit our Committee Minutes Library for minutes from previous Planning Applications Committees.

We aim to make a decision within 13 weeks for major developments and eight weeks for minor and other developments. If you disagree with our decision, you can appeal via the Planning Portal website.

 

How do I view or comment on a planning application?

Visit our Online Registers for Planning and Building Control page to view and comment on current applications.

 

What should I do if I'm affected by a planning proposal?

If you are worried about excessive traffic, the size of a new build, or a potential loss of daylight or privacy due to a new development, you should submit a comment on the planning application. Contact Planning Aid for free if you need advice and cannot afford a planning consultant.

You can also raise concerns over potential health risks of a telecommunications development. However, if the developer proves the plans meet EU recommendations, we normally will not object. 

Where can I see your planning policies?

Visit our planning policies page for a list of policies we might apply to your planning application.

What happens if I see someone breaching planning policy?

Download our  Planning Enforcement Policy PDF [267kb] to see how we deal with breaches of planning control. You can use our online reporting form to report breaches of planning control.

How do you protect our natural environment and historical sites?

We look to protect places of local interested wherever possible. Download our  draft heritage statement [345kb] to see how you can comment on our local heritage.

We seek advice from Berkshire Archaeology if we think archaeological sites or monuments might be damaged during a development. We have also established a number of conservation areas. Download our  conservation areas map [86kb] to see if your plans are in one of these areas - you can download  details of our individual conservation areas [19Mb] for more information. You will need to apply for conservation area consent on the Planning Portal website if you want to build in these areas and must give us six weeks' written notice if you want to carry out work on any trees in a conservation area.

If you are planning to alter or demolish a listed building, you will need to apply for listed building consent on the Planning Portal website as well as planning permission for the new building. We also have a  list of locally important buildings [79kb] which we are unlikely to let you alter.
