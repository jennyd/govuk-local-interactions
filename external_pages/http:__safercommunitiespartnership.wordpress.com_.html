Reporting speeding traffic made easier by road safety partnership

Leave a reply

It is now easier for the public to report road safety concerns, with a quicker, more customer-focused process meaning residents will be kept up to date throughout as their complaint is dealt with.

This comes as the 95 Alive York and North Yorkshire Road Safety Partnership launches a new process for the “speed management protocol”.

With the establishment of a central police traffic bureau, there is now a “one-stop shop” to which a simple completed form can be sent. This will give the partnership all the information it needs to investigate each report. For the first time, the form will be available to be completed online.

The investigations and decision making will be carried out locally by a group of road safety, police, fire and community safety officers working together to assess the speed and traffic data they collect over a complete week and see whether anything can be done to improve matters.

The Chair of the 95 Alive Road Safety Partnership, Deputy Chief Constable Tim Madgwick, said:

“Our well established partnership approach to road safety in North Yorkshire and York brings together expertise and resources from the various agencies that are all involved in making our roads safer for everyone to use. It makes sense for us to provide a single central address for our residents and communities to contact that will follow up their concerns directly with them.”

North Yorkshire County Councillor Don Mackenzie, Executive Member for Road Safety, said: “We are most grateful to the police for offering this central support service to provide a single place to collate and progress these speed concern reports. We know that traffic and speed are very real concerns for our residents and our working together in this way means that just one report to one email or postal address will set an investigation going.”

The Police and Crime Commissioner, Julia Mulligan, added: “Speeding and road safety continue to be the community safety issue which is most often raised with me. I warmly welcome this new, improved service, which means the public now have an easy avenue to report their concerns and be kept up to date on what the 95 Alive partnership can do to help. Having a ‘one-stop shop’ will also make the process quicker, which I know the public will appreciate.”

Executive Member for Road Safety In York, City Councillor Ian Gillies, said: “I hear many complaints and concerns about traffic and speeding from our residents. It is good that we can direct them to a simple, straightforward way of reporting their concerns to us. When a site has been investigated, the information will be there on the website for everyone to see.”

More information and advice on keeping safe on North Yorkshire’s roads and the speed concern report form can be found at www.northyorkshire.police.uk/reportspeeding and www.roadwise.co.uk/using-the-road/speed-concerns. It can also be found by searching for “speed concerns” on the North Yorkshire County Council website, www.northyorks.gov.uk, and the City of York Council website, www.york.gov.uk.

Hate Crime Awareness Week – agencies join forces

Leave a reply

A survey is being launched in an effort to establish the level of hate crime in the Scarborough borough and encourage more victims to come forward.

Agencies believe it goes largely unreported and are launching the survey to coincide with the start of Hate Crime Awareness Week which begins this Saturday (10 October). The survey – aimed at victims of hate crime or those who have witnessed it – can be completed at https://www.surveymonkey.com/r/PMBVJ8N

Hate crime is defined as “a crime the victim or any other person perceives to be motivated by hostility or prejudice towards any aspect of a person’s identity”, such as disability, gender identity, race, ethnicity or nationality, religion, faith or belief, or sexual orientation.

North Yorkshire County Council Community Cohesion Worker, Julie Hay, who is co-ordinating public engagement in the town during Hate Crime Awareness Week, on behalf of a number of local agencies and groups, said: “We know from research nationally and locally that people frequently do not report when they are victims or witnesses of hate crimes.

“The local survey is designed to help us establish the extent of hate crime experienced by people in the Scarborough borough and why they do not always report it. This will then help us to deal with the issue better in the future.”

Scarborough Borough Council Community Safety and Safeguarding Manager, Sandra Rees, who has also been involved in planning the week of public engagement, added: “I’d always urge anyone who has experienced a hate crime to report it so they can get access to the support they may need.”

The survey will be launched at a special briefing at 10am on Saturday at The Hub in Northway, the campaign centre for the week. It will include a talk by a representative of Stop Hate UK on what hate crime is, its impact on communities and the service it offers. The event will be supported by the Mayor of the Borough of Scarborough, Cllr Tom Fox.

Volunteers, along with professionals from the county council, police, borough council, Community Impact Team, Coast and Vale Community Action (CAVCA), University of Hull Scarborough Campus, Scarborough Equality and Diversity Group, and Stop Hate UK will split their time between The Hub and the town centre.

The volunteers and professionals will be provided with information about hate crimes and incidents, the services and support available, and the new local survey. The team will also distribute Stop Hate UK and Supporting Victims material and local survey postcards and will engage with members of the public to discuss issues relating to hate crime.

It is envisaged that some hate incidents may be reported on the day to members of the team. These will be directed to the PCSOs to obtain full details and recorded in line with North Yorkshire Police policy.

Throughout the remainder of the week police staff and partners will continue distributing Stop Hate UK posters across the wider area and will encourage as many Scarborough borough residents to complete the local survey as possible.

Anyone who has been the victim of a hate crime can call 101 to speak to local police, phone Stop Hate UK on 0800 1381625 or go to their website, www.stophateuk.uk. People can also contact Supporting Victims on 01904 669276 or go to www.supportingvictims.org

Legal highs day of action

Leave a reply

Scarborough’s multi-agency Community Impact Team has staged a “day of action” centred around so-called legal highs.

Police, borough council community safety officers and drugs workers took to the streets and visited schools to talk to people and handed out information about legal highs, properly known as new psychoactive substances (NPS).

Chief Inspector Lindsey Stamp, Deputy Commander covering Scarborough and Ryedale, said: “We wanted to get the message across to people that even though a substance is being sold as ‘legal’, it doesn’t mean it’s safe or legal. Users can’t really be sure of what’s in a ‘legal high’ or what its affects might be.

“Communities are suffering because of new psychoactive substances and we are determined to do all we can, with the help of our partners, to discourage people from using and take action against premises which supply NPS, where that’s possible.”

‘Legal highs’ often contain a mixture of chemical ingredients intended to produce similar effects to illegal drugs like cocaine, cannabis and ecstasy. They’re sold in powder form and as pills, liquids, capsules, perforated tabs and smoking mixtures.

Some of the effects linked to psychoactive substances include breathing difficulties, heart palpitations, paranoia, psychosis, hallucinations, seizures and comas and it’s thought ‘legal highs’ have contributed to more than 100 deaths in the UK since 2009.

The day of action was part of an ongoing campaign called Legal Highs, Lethal Lows, which is supported by the Home Office and backed by a local parents’ action group. It ties in with Scarborough Borough Council’s New Psychoactive Substances Strategy and Action Plan.

The council’s Community Safety and Safeguarding Manager, Sandra Rees, said: “The day of action was mainly about giving people the chance to talk to us about ‘legal highs’ and offering help and support.

“It tied in with the excellent work our partners are already doing in schools and the enforcement action we’ve taken and will continue to take against a number of suppliers who sell ‘legal highs’.”

People wanting to talk to someone about legal highs can call Compass REACH on the freephone number, 0800 0087452 or visit www.talktofrank.com or www.northyorks.gov.uk/drugs for further information.
