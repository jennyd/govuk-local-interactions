Tree protection

Trees are an important part of Herefordshire's environment. They perform a variety of functions in the urban and rural landscape, providing visual amenity, biodiversity, historical heritage and sustainability. Woodlands also benefit the local economy, tourism, and leisure. They generally improve our lives and provide extensive benefits to the global environment.

Herefordshire Council is committed to the preservation and sustainable management of trees, and we aim to enhance and develop the resource. Due toÂ their importance, planning legislation protects trees.

View Tree Preservation Orders (TPOs) on the administrative map

What are Tree Preservation Orders (TPOs)?

There are about 470 Tree Preservation Orders (TPOs) in Herefordshire, covering approximately 743,000 individual trees. The orders cover individual trees, groups of trees, areas of trees and woodlands.

The 1990 Town and Country Planning Act places a duty on local planning authorities to make TPOs to preserve trees or woodlands in their area. We regularly make new orders to protect trees and we have a standard tree evaluation system to assess them. This assessment takes account of:

  • Landscape importance and visibility
  • Aesthetic value
  • Intrinsic beauty
  • Relation to setting
  • The condition and form of the tree

The government advises local planning authorities to keep their TPOs under review in order to ensure they are accurate, relevant and defensible. We are, therefore, in the continual and long-term process of carrying out a review of our TPOs.

How are trees assessed in planning applications?

You should submit a tree survey for trees within or adjacent to a planning application site. An arboriculturist should carry out the survey and record information about the trees on a site. The plans and particulars should be in accordance with British Standard 5837: 2012 - Trees in relation to design, demolition and construction - Recommendations. A summary of our requirements is in the guidance tree surveys and protection during development (PDF document).

We will assess the impact of the development on the trees, and weÂ may protect them by a tree preservation order, or by a condition to ensure their protection during development and future retention.

When should I apply for a felling licence?

Under the Forestry Act 1967, you must obtain a felling licence from the Forestry Commission if you are, in any calendar quarter, either felling more than five cubic metres of wood, or selling more than two cubic metres of wood.

You can email the Forestry Commission officers at westmidlands.fce@forestry.gsi.gov.uk

FAQs

Why is the council cutting down an apparently healthy tree in my street?

We carry out regular and full safety surveys for street and park trees across the county to find out if maintenance work is needed to ensure the tree does not pose a threat to public safety. This survey is carried out by a registered Arboricultural Association consultant.

Often routine maintenance work is required to extend the life of the tree. This work includes:

  • Canopy reduction
  • Thinning
  • Removal of dead wood and broken branches

Some trees can appear healthy to the untrained eye even when they have been structurally weakened by decay from fungal infections. For health and safety reasons these trees are not considered acceptable in a public area. If a tree poses a severe threat to the public then we must remove it.
