Dropped kerb

  • a dropped kerb allows vehicles to cross the pavement from the road to a driveway
  • check what you'll need to know from our Planning Department before making an application
  • read our guidance notes before making an application

Dropped kerbs

What is a dropped kerb?

It is illegal for a vehicle to be driven over the kerb, footway or verge where there is no proper vehicle crossing in place. If you wish to park your car on your property then a proper crossing needs to be installed, this involves dropping (lowering) the kerb and strengthening the pavement in front of the property.

In order to ensure that vehicle crossings are properly constructed, all domestic dropped kerbs will be built by the council. Dropped kerbs add value to the property and therefore will not be funded from the council's public funds. It is also sometimes necessary for street furniture (lamp-posts, man-hole covers etc) to be moved.

However, if you are registered disabled then you can approach the council's Occupational Therapy team on 0300 300 8303. 

Please see additional information on parking next to dropped kerbs.

Apply for a dropped kerb

Before applying for a dropped kerb, you must contact the Planning Department (Email: planning@centralbedfordshire.gov.uk) to check if planning permission is required. It is essential that you know who owns any land between your property and the highway and whether any other third party has rights or covenants over any land that you propose to cross.

Once you have contacted the Planning Department and read the guidance notes (PDF 91KB), you can make an online application for a dropped kerb. 

Remember, it costs £121 to make an application, which is non-refundable. This will cover the costs of a site visit, design and quotation for implementing the dropped kerb. We will then provide you with a quotation for the installation. Before going ahead with your application, you can check the full costs for dropped kerbs (PDF 108KB).

The following is a list of conditions relevant to the construction and the use of a domestic dropped kerb:

Vehicle size: it may not be used by heavy goods vehicles or mechanical equipment. If a delivery, such as a skip, is made into the property, and in doing so the delivery damages the crossing, any repairs will be the responsibility of the occupier.

Size of the crossing: the crossing will be constructed to dimensions that are suitable for vehicles to access the property. A crossing which covers the full frontage will not be permitted except in exceptional circumstances.

Shared access: where the occupiers of two adjoining properties share a driveway, and wish to build a double width crossing to serve the two sites, one occupier should act on behalf of both parties.

Parking provision: your application may not be approved unless you are able to provide a suitable parking area within your property, this must be at least 4.8 metres long, measured from the front of your house to the boundary of your property and 2.44 metres wide. There must be enough space around this area for pedestrian access. If the width of your property is more than 8m these conditions may not apply.

Our decision as to whether the application will be approved or refused is final.

Hard standing: where you are intending to use gravel or a similar loose material for your hard standing, you should consider the problem of material being carried on to the highway by the movement of the vehicle. This is especially true where the surface comes up to the boundary. Where material of this type is used, concrete or blacktop should be laid in a 500mm strip from the boundary to the start of the gravelled area. This will help to reduce any problem. If the material is carried onto the highway it will be the responsibility of the occupier to remove it by sweeping.

No part of a vehicle parked within your property may project on to or over the highway (road, pavement or grass verge). The crossing may not be used as a parking area.

Drainage: the parking area within your property must be built so that water does not drain from it across the footway. Suitable drainage must be provided within the boundaries of your property. 

Street furniture: where applicants have removed part of the wall or fence running along the property boundary, that is greater than required for the size of the crossing, it should be understood that an item of street furniture, i.e. lamp post, telegraph pole, traffic sign etc., may be erected at any time in the footway outside the area of a crossing.

Obstacles to construction: if the proposed position of the access is obstructed by a road sign, lamp post, or tree, etc. the location should be altered to avoid the obstacle. If this is not feasible, a decision will have to be made by Central Bedfordshire Council as to whether the item should be removed or relocated.

If a statutory authority is required to carry out work by relocating a fire hydrant, telegraph pole etc. any charges for such work will be the responsibility of the applicant, who will be required to produce written proof of approval by the authority to Central Bedfordshire Council before a crossing can be built.

If an applicant wishes the crossing to be placed in a location and this requires the relocation of a lamp post or similar item, he/she will be required to pay the full cost of relocation.

Alterations: Central Bedfordshire Council may need to alter the layout of your vehicle crossover at any time, due to modifications in the footway or verge. Every effort will be made to maintain access to your property and the occupier of premises affected will be given adequate notice of such works.

Safety: any application for the construction of a domestic crossing may be refused or modified on the grounds of safety. The applicant must ensure that adequate sight lines are maintained to allow safe access to their property.

Gates: gates fitted across the vehicle entrance to your property should, in no circumstances, open outwards across the footpath or carriageway.
