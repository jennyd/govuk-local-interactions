The Care Act 2014

Many of us will need care and support at some point in our lives, and most people will pay at least something towards the cost of their care. From April 2015, the government is bringing in a new piece of legislation called the Care Act. If you receive care and support, or you support someone as a carer, you could benefit from the changes.

This Act will help to put you more in control of the help and support you receive. Any decisions about your care and support will consider your wellbeing and what is important to you and your family, so you can stay healthy and remain independent for longer.

  • Department of Health factsheets about the Care Act

What is the Care Act changing?

Needs

For the first time, there will be a national level of care and support needs that all local authorities will consider when they assess what help they can give you. Care plans will be tailored to your needs and will work out how you can do the things that are important to you and your family. You will also know how much it will cost to meet your needs and how much the local authority will contribute towards the cost. You will have more control over how that money is spent.

If you decide to move to another area, local authorities will also have to work together to make sure that there is no gap in your care when you move.

If the support you need could be met by local community services organised by local charities or other support networks, we will put you in touch with the right organisation to support your wellbeing and help you remain independent for longer.

More information on your needs under the Care Act.

New rights and support for carers

You are a carer if you provide help and support, unpaid, to a family member, friend or neighbour who would otherwise not be able to manage. ‘Caring’ for someone covers lots of different things, like helping with their washing, dressing or eating, or taking them to regular appointments.

If this sounds like you, you may be able to get more help so that you can carry on caring and look after your own wellbeing. You may be eligible for support, taken as a personal budget to spend on the things that make caring easier; or practical support, like arranging for someone to step in when you need a short break.

You may prefer to be put in touch with local support groups so you have people to talk to. We can help you find the right support and may suggest that you have a carer’s assessment. A carer’s assessment will look at the different ways that caring affects your life and work out how you can carry on doing the things that are important to you and your family.

More information on new rights and support for carers under the Care Act.

Information and advice

The Care Act says we must ensure people have good information and advice on how to get the right support to stay happy and independent. If people need extra support, they need to know what choices there are and what the next step is.

Local authorities are also now required to tell people how to get independent financial information and to support those who need it with an independent advocate.

If you would like to see our plans for developing an information and advice service in Hertfordshire take a look at our information and advice strategy. If you have any feedback on our current information and advice, or have a good idea for doing things differently, we want to hear from you: please email sharon.robinson@hertfordshire.gov.uk.

Market shaping

Local authorities must influence the development of care and support services to ensure a wide range of good quality services and to give people more choice and control. To find out more, take a look at our market position statements.

Protecting adults from abuse or neglect (safeguarding)

Adult safeguarding an important part of what many public services do, but the key responsibility is with local authorities in partnership with the police and the NHS. The Care Act puts adult safeguarding on a legal footing and from April 2015 tells us what we must do around:

  • Making enquiries if an adult is subject to, or at risk of, abuse or neglect
  • How to run our Safeguarding Adults Board
  • When to offer people an independent advocate
  • How to cooperate with our partners in order to protect adults experiencing, or at risk of, abuse or neglect.

Find out more about adult safeguarding in Hertfordshire.

Paying for care

Deferred payments

A deferred payment agreement is an arrangement with the council that allows people to use the value of their homes to help pay care home costs. The Care Act says all local authorities must now offer deferred payments. Find out more about deferred payments in Hertfordshire.

Cap on care costs (was from April 2016) This change has been postponed until 2020 by the Government, to allow time to be taken to ensure that everyone is ready to introduce the new system and to look at what more can be done to support people with the costs of care.

At the moment there is no limit to what care and support can cost, and this means that people with very high care needs may have to pay expensive bills. From 2020 there will be a new form of protection from unlimited costs. This protection is called the ‘cap on care costs.’

It means that no one will have to pay more than £72,000 towards the care element of the costs of meeting their eligible needs in their lifetime, and many people will pay much less. This applies to people funding their own care and support, as well as those helped by the council.

Alongside the cap on care costs, extended financial support will ensure that more people are eligible for help with care and support costs. The council will assess your finances and we may be able to offer extra help if you cannot afford to pay. Most people will still have to contribute something towards the cost of their care and support.

More information on paying for care under the Care Act.

What is important to people?

It was a top priority for us to ask service users and carers what they thought about our services and how they would like things to be different in the future. We held a range of involvement activities, including workshops for people with learning disabilities and carers.

People told us that it is critical to take into account the role of family carers and use of emergency planning; the importance of clear and consistent information and advice; and how they want greater flexibility to use Direct Payments.

You can read more in our stakeholder conference report and the themes from our Care Act engagement.
