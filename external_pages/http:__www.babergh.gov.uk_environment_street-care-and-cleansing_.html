Street Care and Cleansing

An efficient and effective street cleansing service is essential to provide the kind of environment that we would like to live in. The quality of the local environment, and in particular standards of street cleansing, are increasingly becoming the measure that the public uses to judge how well an area is being managed and its suitability as a place to live, work, visit or bring up a family. 

Our aim is to keep the District a clean, environmentally safe, well managed and pleasant place to be.

Cleaner streets cannot be achieved solely through better cleansing - we need people to take responsibility for litter and waste in the first place - and where this doesn't happen we will seek to take robust enforcement action.

How often do streets get cleaned?

Babergh District Council carries out routine and reactive street cleaning of adopted roads and footpaths within towns and villages throughout the district. Different areas of the districts are cleaned on different frequencies, but as a general rule, town centres are cleaned every day and rural areas as and when required.
It is important to note however, that our schedules are flexible and we do not clean a street that does not need cleaning. The emphasis of the Council is to do our best to keep the district clean. That means that if you report areas (or we find areas) that have become a problem, we will deal with them rather than sticking to a schedule just because it exists.

 

Does the Council sweep roads?

The Council is responsible for the scheduled mechanical sweeping of all kerbed and drained roads within the district. This is generally performed on a quarterly or half-yearly basis according to need.

Who do I report a problem with a dog or litter bin to?

The majority of dog and litter bins across the district are supplied and maintained by the local town or parish council.  The street cleansing team empty bins mostly on a weekly basis, with bins in busy areas emptied more frequently.  Requests for new or replacement bins should be directed to the appropriate town or parish council.

Do the Councils collect dead animals?

All dead wild and domestic animals on the highway are collected by the street cleansing team. All domestic animals are scanned for identification chips and, wherever possible, owners are contacted.

More information on Do the Councils collect dead animals?

Is litter cleared from A roads?

Road side litter is one of the fastest growing litter problems. The high levels and speed of traffic on A classification roads makes clearing litter a dangerous proposition. The Environmental Protection Act 1990 makes allowance for this and as such litter clearance that might require road closure is to be performed when practicable and preferably incorporated in to other scheduled works. The litter clearance work for both Councils on the A14 & A12  is covered part of a partnership scheme with Ipswich Borough Council.

Are the Councils responsible for emergency clearance?

Suffolk County Council is responsible for any emergency clearing of materials, including mud, that might be a hazard on the highway and storm water drains.

More information on Are the Councils responsible for emergency clearance?

What does the council do about graffiti?

There is a common misconception that councils are responsible for clearing up graffiti and that this falls within the street cleansing service.  This is not the case - graffiti removal is the responsibility of whosever property it is on, it is criminal damage and should be dealt with as such.  The only graffiti that the council is responsible for clearing is that on its own property. Where the defacement is on private property, the council encourages property owners to remove graffiti.  However, if the defacement is offensive, the Councils are happy to work with the owners to remove the defacement as quickly as possible.

More information on What does the council do about graffiti?

Who is responsible for clearing fly-tipped waste?

The Council and the Environment Agency are responsible for clearing up/investigating fly-tipping on public land.  Clearing fly-tipping from private land is the responsibility of the landowner.

More information on Who is responsible for clearing fly-tipped waste?

What should I expect from the Council in respect of standards and response times?

The Council’s legal duty is set out in the Government's Code of Practice for litter, and this describes expected response times for different land use types.

How much does the service cost?

The overall cost of delivering Babergh's street cleansing service is around 475k per year. This is divided up as follows:

  • Routine cleansing of urban parishes - £265k (includes £80k to cleanse Sudbury town centre and £50k to cleanse Hadleigh town centre)
  • Routine cleansing of rural parishes - £61k
  • Litter and dog waste bin emptying - £72k (part-funded by town & parish councils)
  • Reactive cleansing (including fly-tipping removal) - £45k
  • Trunk road (A12 & A14) litter picking - £32k

Does the Council clear leaves?

The removal of fallen leaves on public highways and Babergh grounds is included as part of the routine street cleansing and maintenance of these areas. Where we receive a report of fallen leaves between routine cleansing of these areas, we will endeavour to arrange an additional cleanse of these, with the priority being leaves which have broken down into detritus.

We do not clear leaves from private property (even if the leaves have fallen from Babergh trees) nor do we clear leaves from grass areas - the only exception to this rule are Babergh parks and Babergh sheltered housing sites, where leaves are routinely cleared from the whole site, including grass areas, on a weekly basis.
