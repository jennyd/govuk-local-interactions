Using Your Recycling Collection Service

|What happens to my recycling?

Every fortnight we collect your recycling from your blue top wheeled bin. This is on the alternate week to your landfill (rubbish) collection. We collect a wide range of items for recycling including a wider rage of plastics since 2011. You can watch the short film below to find out more about which plastics you can recycle.

Recycling Video (|Recycling Video Transcript)
First Available 6/12/2011

|How to use the recycling service

  • Only place loose recyclable materials, individually into your bin
  • Do not place them in a plastic bag as these cannot be recycled
  • Cardboard items that will not easily fit in your recycling bin can be flattened, secured as a bundle that can be easily picked up by a collector and left next to the bin.
  • On collection day, place your recycling bin at the boundary of your property
  • |See a full list of what you can put into your recycling bin here

|Top tips for recycling

  • Remove plastic lids from containers and put them in your recycling bin separately
  • Rinse out containers
  • Recycle meat trays and other food trays but remove any plastic film first
  • Take juice cartons, called Tetra-Paks, to your local recycling centre where Tetra-Pak have their own collection bank
  • Keep a separate bin or box indoors to store recyclable material
  • Flatten cardboard boxes
  • Sign up to the |Mailing Preference Service to stop unwanted mail from being put through you door
  • You can also opt out of unaddressed mail using the |Royal Mail opt out service
  • If something can't be put in your recycling bin, check our |Recycling A to Z to see if it can be reused, recycled or taken to a |Recycling Centre.

|Recycling Small Electrical Items

Anything with a plug or battery can be recycled from irons and radios to microwaves and computers.

Where can I recycle my electric and electronic items?

  • Put them out to be collected by the British Heart Foundation |Clothes+ team 4 times annually.
  • Drop them in one of the WEEE banks found at your Local Recycling Banks. |Find your nearest one here.
  • Take them to a |Community Recycling Centre (the tip)
  • Retailer 'Take Back' schemes - for a list of retailers that will take back electrical and electronic equipment, go to the |Recycle Now A-Z of retailers
  • Advertise it on a reuse network like |Freecycle which pass unwanted items on for free. Visit the |Recycle More website for a list of other sites.
  • Check with friends and family to see if anyone could make good use of it.

Why should we recycle electrical and electronic items?
Electrical items are purchased in their millions in the UK each year, and over 75% of waste electrical goods end up in landfill. This is an enormous amount of wasted items that could have been repaired or recycled. Alternatively they can be taken apart for their precious finite metals that are in more limited supply. (Even broken items). These parts are valuable in the recycling process and can be reused in new products.

In January 2007 the |WEEE directive came into force to encourage the re-use and recycling of electrical and electronic items and to prevent them from going to landfill. Under the directive, the manufacturers and suppliers have to comply with certain regulations which means that they take responsibility for the items that they produce. Since July 2007, they have had to either offer in store take back schemes for unwanted electrical goods or register on a producer compliance scheme to cover the waste costs of their goods.

|Recycling for businesses?

If you're a business and your interested in adopting greener practises visit the |Surrey Green Steps to find out how.

|What happens to my recycling?

After we pick up your recycling, it is taken to Grundons Materials Recycling Facility (MRF) in Leatherhead.

The recycling first passes through a pre-treatment room and then travels via conveyors into the main sorting area. Glass is removed before this happens. Aluminium and steel cans are removed by a mechanical process. The remaining recyclables are hand picked in the main sorting area. All recyclables except glass are then baled and sent to the re-processors.

|Paper and Cardboard
Baled paper is made into pulp. Contaminants, like staples, are removed and the fibres are cleaned. The pulp is then made into new paper products such as newspapers, magazines and toilet rolls. Cardboard is generally recycled into further cardboard products.

Aluminium and steel cans from local recycling banks are re-processed in Wales and are shredded and fed into a magnetic separator to remove any bits of steel, like ring pulls. The metal is then blasted with hot air (to remove the lacquer which covers the surface) and melted down. The molten metal is |turned into new cans. |Steel is melted down in large furnaces and poured into moulds to make new drink cans and car parts.

|Plastic
Plastics are reformed into moulding granules that are washed and dried. This is then mixed with additives and/or more virgin raw material, extruded and chopped into pellets ready to be turned into new plastic products.

|Glass
If glass is mixed colours it is ground down, crushed into eco-sand and used as aggregate in the laying of roads. If the glass is separated into individual colours it's taken to a glass bottle re-processor. There, the glass is crushed into 'cullet' and passed through a magnet, vacuum and laser to remove any contaminants. It's then melted and |made into new glass.

Clothes, Textiles and Shoes
Clothes collected from our textile banks are sorted, reused and recycled by the British Heart Foundation. The clothes we dispose of are often made of high quality material. These goods are welcomed in their charity shops where they are sold to raise funds. Anything unsuitable for sale is sent for recycling - which may involve the items being broken down into fibres and made into new material.
