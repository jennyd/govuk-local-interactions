Licensing Act 2003

What does the act do?

The Licensing Act 2003 modernised licensing legislation and gives greater freedom and more responsibility to the leisure and hospitality industry and their customers. The greater freedom for the leisure and hospitality industry has been balanced by introducing tougher powers for police, courts and councils.

The law is designed to meet the government's four licensing objectives and all decisions about licences are based on these four objectives:

  • The prevention of crime and disorder

  • Public safety
  • The prevention of public nuisance
  • The protection of children from harm

 The act introduced a single integrated system which regulates

  • the sale or supply of alcohol
  • the provision of regulated entertainment or entertainment facilities
  • the provision of late night hot food or drink (after 11pm)

Who is affected?

The Licensing Act 2003 affects anyone concerned with:

  • Pubs and nightclubs

  • Indoor sporting events
  • Off licences
  • Restaurants that serve alcohol
  • Businesses offering hot foods or drinks between 11.00pm and 5.00am
  • Hotels and guest houses and other places that serve alcohol
  • Private members' clubs and social clubs
  • Theatres and amateur dramatic groups
  • Cinemas
  • Organisers of occasional entertainment
  • Live music performances

Which licence do you need?

To supply or sell alcohol, provide entertainment or entertainment facilities or provide late night refreshment, you must have a premises licence.

Every premises where alcohol is supplied under the authority of a premises licence requires a  designated premises supervisor (Word/32.79 KB), a personal licence (Except for qualifying clubs).

Applicants for premises licences and club premises certificates must advertise the application on the premises and in a local newspaper to allow any person to make representations.

You can also view the  frequently asked and answers regarding the Licensing Act 2003 (WORD/339KB/9pages).

You can also view a list of criminal offences under the Licensing Act (PDF, 44KB)

Fees

Fees for premises licences, club premises certificates, personal licences and temporary event notices have been set by the Government, the local council has no control over the setting of such fees

A fee is payable upon application for a premises licence and club premises certificate and this is followed by an annual maintenance fee. It is based on the non-domestic rateable value of the premises, which is not the same as the amount of non-domestic rates payable each year.

The cost of a personal licence is £37.

You can view all licensing fees.

Live Music Act 2012

Under the Live Music Act 2012 live music will cease to be regulated entertainment in venues licensed for the sale of alcohol for consumption on the premises in the following situations:

  • when it is unamplified and takes place between 8am and 11pm

  • when it is amplified and takes place in the presence of an audience of 200 persons or less and is provided between 8am and 11pm.

The premises must be open for the sale of alcohol during the time that the live music is provided for the exemption/s to take effect.

Additionally, any condition attached to the Premises Licence relating to live music ceases to have effect in respect of the live music unless the council states otherwise on the licence pursuant to a Review Hearing.

For those premises which are not licensed for alcohol sales, the act states that live music is not regulated entertainment when it is unamplified and takes place between 8am and 11pm.

Amplified music in non-licensed venues will still require formal authorisation from the council, such as a premises licence or temporary event notice.

The act also removes the need to license entertainment entertainment facilities. This means dance floors, microphone stands ad pianos etc.

South Lakes best practice guidance

The supervision and enforcement of activities associated with premises licences within South Lakeland is the shared partnership responsibility between officers from South Lakeland District Council (as the licensing authority), Cumbria Constabulary, Cumbria Fire and Rescue and most importantly the premises licence holder or designated premises supervisor (DPS).

Our partnership policy is to work for compliance and seek best working practices. Only when this has failed, or due to circumstances, is likely to fail, will we resort to legal action.

Using the  South Lakes best practice guidance (PDF, 924 Kb) will help you to deliver responsible management of your premises in line with the licensing objectives.  Should you require further advice, contact any partner agencies outlined in the guidance document.
