City Residents' Meetings

The annual City-wide Residents’ Meeting was held in the Old Library at Guildhall on Monday 6 July 2015. The meetings offered residents the chance to he​ar from, and ask questions to, elected Members and senior officers from the City Corporation and the City of London Police​. This year we included talks on air pollution and crime and safety.

The meeting presentations (1MB) are now available as are minutes (142 KB) from the meeting. The latter includes details from the question and answer session from both sessions.

City stakeholder survey

Every three years the City Corporation commissions four separate surveys with residents, workers, businesses and senior executives. Along with existing feedback methods, the results from this polling help us shape our future priorities and demonstrate how we listen to our different audiences.

These surveys aim to discover satisfaction levels with the City as a place to live, work and do business. They also test satisfaction with the City Corporation and its services, tracking satisfaction levels over time and enabling comparison with London local authorities.

Key findings 2013 summary

Attitudes to the City of London

  • Satisfaction with the City of London as a place to live/work/run a business remains high, with over nine out of ten satisfied with the local area across all four audiences.
  • Traffic congestion and public transport/commuting are seen as key priorities by both businesses and workers to improve the City of London as a place to work and do business.

Attitudes to the City Corporation

  • Audiences are satisfied with the way the City Corporation runs things with resident satisfaction at 87%, senior executives 85%, workers 75% and businesses 69%. No significant shifts in satisfaction since the previous survey in 2009.
  • The perception of ‘value for money’ (this is a new question) follows a similar trend: residents 73%, senior executives 61%, workers 49% and businesses 40%.
  • Familiarity with the City Corporation is highest amongst residents 67%, senior executives 51%, workers 36% and businesses 29%. The business figure shows a slight fall from the 39% measured in 2009.
  • One explanation could be the increased number of new businesses in the City, and the correlation between familiarity with the City Corporation and the number of years a business has been established here. Among businesses that have been in the City for less than 5 years, only 12% felt that they knew the City Corporation very well whereas 44% felt they did not know it at all.
  • When asked whether the City Corporation’s role in promoting the City and representing its interests at home and abroad should be increased, decreased or remain the same: 59% of senior executives and 61% of businesses felt it should be increased, 39% of senior executives and 31% of businesses felt it should remain the same. No senior executives and only 1% of businesses felt it should be decreased. 87% of senior executives felt that the role of the Lord Mayor was very relevant or fairly relevant.

City Competitiveness

  • New York remains the main competitive challenge to the City of London: 41% of senior executives consider it ‘a great challenge’. Asian markets are also starting to be seen as a competitive challenge, with many senior executives expecting their firms to conduct more business in the next five years, in particular with Hong Kong (27%) and Singapore (37%).
  • Regulation is the main issue executives feel may jeopardise the City’s long term status as a leading global financial centre, with 72% mentioning European regulation (an increase of 21% from 2009) and 65% mentioning UK regulation. Taxation is also seen as a concern by nearly half (46%), but less so than in 2009 (down 14%).
  • There has been a significant rise in the number of senior executives who feel that immigration controls/visa issues could jeopardise the City’s long term status – up 9% to 13%.

Policy issues

  • In response to the question ‘If Britain was to vote to leave the EU, what impact would that have on your business here in the City of London?’ 69% of senior executives feel that it would have a negative impact on their business. Only 9% feels the impact would be positive, 14% no impact and 8% did not know.
  • 23% of senior executives are satisfied with current arrangements for businesses visitor visas for non-EU citizens, compared to 47% who are dissatisfied. 23% were neutral and 7% did not know.
