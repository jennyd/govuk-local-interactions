What is the Care Act? 

The Care Act 2014 represents the most significant reform of care and support in more than 60 years. The Act has combined various existing pieces of legislation, which were previously used to shape the social care system in England.

The Act is being introduced in two stages. The care and support reforms come into place in April 2015. Further changes will come into place in April 2016.

The Care Act changes many aspects of how support is arranged, and aims to give greater control and influence to those who receive support. Some of the most significant developments are:

  • changes to when and how people will be asked to contribute towards the cost of support
  • a single national eligibility criteria for receiving support
  • a greater emphasis on protecting the most vulnerable people in our society from abuse and neglect
  • a greater emphasis on local authorities to provide information and advice about health and financial support
  • everyone who is assessed as having eligible unmet needs for care and support will receive a personal budget as part of the support plan
  • for local authorities to provide an advocate to people who need someone to speak on their behalf
  • a change in the way local authorities approach assessments for those in need of support. People requiring help will be encouraged to think about what outcomes they want to achieve in their lives. These outcomes will be changes which improve their physical or emotional well-being
  • a greater emphasis on prevention. Local authorities and other providers of support, will encourage and assist people to lead healthy lives, which will reduce the chances of later requiring care
  • greater regulation for those who provide professional care and support, and tougher penalties for those who do not provide care and support of a high enough standard
  • new rights for carers, which put them on the same footing as the people they care for. Carers who feel they may need support should request a Carers Assessment. If a carer is eligible for support for particular needs, they will have a legal right to receive support for those needs, just like the people they care for

The government has produced a series of fact-sheets which will help to explain the aims of the Care Act. There is also an easy-read version.

Other information and advice

The Skills for Care website have produced various resources which explain the main aims of the Care Act. Although aimed mainly at care professionals, they also serve as a good introduction to the Act for members of the public. See: Care Act 2014.

The Local Government Association have produced a fact-sheet called Get in on the Act, with a summary of all the main changes. They also have a page on their website with a brief summary of these changes.
